## 1. Claim Under Pressure

Branching threatens singular same-self owned continuity even when memory, pattern, narrative, and behavioral continuity are preserved in multiple successors.

Stronger claims not assumed:

- branching does not show that successors lack consciousness
- branching does not make pattern continuity worthless
- branching does not settle personal identity
- branching does not establish OCH
- non-branching continuity is not yet established as necessary

## 2. Definitions From PRIMITIVES.md

- **branching:** a continuity threat where one prior stream yields multiple equally valid successor streams
- **ownership:** the mark by which an experience, action, body-state, memory, or perspective is mine
- **continuity:** identity-bearing preservation through time
- **owned continuity:** selfhood as a continuity-bearing ownership-stream inside a conscious field
- **identity preservation:** transformed continuity remaining same-self rather than copy, fork, role, reconstruction, or replacement
- **memory residue:** remembered or reportable trace left by a prior state or transition
- **pattern continuity:** preservation of informational, functional, behavioral, or structural similarity
- **successor:** a later system or stream inheriting some prior structure, memory, pattern, or causal relation
- **singular continuation:** one prior ownership-stream continuing as one successor without symmetry-breaking branch competition
- **copy/fork/replacement:** cases where a successor inherits structure without settling whether the prior owner continued

## 3. Prior A5 Anchor

`A_SPLIT_A5_MEMORY_RESIDUE_NOT_CONTINUITY.md` established the current pressure baseline:

- memory is support/residue, not same-self evidence by itself
- pattern similarity is not owner preservation
- narrative continuity is not identity preservation
- branching is the strongest unresolved pressure

The current report sharpens the last point. If memory and pattern can be copied into two successors, then memory and pattern cannot by themselves explain why one successor would be the original owner in a singular sense.

## 5. Non-Branching Constraint

OCH likely needs non-branching as a guardrail, but the current report should not promote it into a complete criterion.

Questions under pressure:

- **Is non-branching necessary?** Possibly for ordinary singular same-self continuity, because equal branches create successor symmetry.
- **Is non-branching sufficient?** No. A single successor can preserve pattern while still failing ownership, field, or causal-process constraints.
- **Does one successor preserve ordinary identity better than two?** Yes as a practical and conceptual default, because it avoids symmetry between competing successor claims.
- **Does destruction of the original matter?** It removes a competing living original, but it does not by itself show transfer rather than replacement.
- **Does gradual transformation avoid the branch problem?** It reduces discontinuity pressure, but if a branch appears during or after gradual replacement, the symmetry problem returns.
- **Can AOC define this without circularity?** Not yet fully. The risk is defining continuity as "whatever preserves the owner" and then using ownership to define continuity.

Best current status:

> Non-branching is a strong guardrail for singular continuation, not a complete theory of identity.

## 6. Successor Symmetry Problem

If successor A and successor B both inherit memory, pattern, and narrative, then the ordinary evidence channels become symmetric.

What could make only one the original owner?

- privileged causal continuity
- uninterrupted dynamical process
- bodily/biological continuity
- non-branching transition history
- an ownership marker not reducible to memory/report/pattern

What if neither uniquely inherits ownership?

- the prior pattern survives as residue in two successors
- two new successor streams may begin or continue from inherited structure
- singular first-person continuation is not secured by the shared evidence

What if both inherit ownership?

- identity becomes plural, branch-relative, or no longer ordinary singular identity
- AOC/OCH would need to revise "same-self" into a branch-relative concept

What if the question has no empirical answer?

- mark it as conceptually underdetermined rather than forcing a fake empirical closure
- keep branch cases as falsifier terrain for any theory that treats memory or pattern as sufficient

## 9. Main Finding

Branching is the strongest pressure against reducing identity to memory, pattern, or narrative continuity. AOC/OCH may need a non-branching owned-stream constraint, but that constraint is not yet established and risks circularity unless operationalized.

The conservative result is not that copies are empty or impossible. The result is that equal successor cases make ordinary singular survival language break: memory can be duplicated, pattern can be duplicated, report can be duplicated, and narrative can be duplicated, while "which one is me?" remains unresolved.
