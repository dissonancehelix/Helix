# Empirical Contacts

Where empirical science can touch, probe, or constrain the Inhabited Continuity Theory. Each entry includes: why it matters, what it could test, and what it cannot prove.

The theory's hardest claims are philosophical primitives — they cannot be directly tested. These entries identify the closest empirical anchors, not confirmations.

---

## No-Report Paradigms

**Why it matters:** The theory distinguishes reportability from owned experience. No-report paradigms — where consciousness is measured without requiring verbal or motor report — directly probe this distinction.

**What it could test:** Whether neural signatures of consciousness (identified in report-based paradigms) persist in non-reporting states. If yes, experience without reportability is empirically tractable. If signatures collapse with reportability, the distinction loses empirical purchase.

**What it cannot prove:** Whether the neural signatures actually correspond to owned experience (the hard problem), or only to processing that would produce report if motor capacity were available.

**Key references:** Lamme's recurrent processing work; Tsuchiya's no-report paradigm studies; hidden consciousness research.

---

## Anesthesia

**Why it matters:** Anesthesia represents disruption of the consciousness/selfhood system at scale. Studying what is disrupted, when, and how provides empirical anchors for which components are load-bearing.

**What it could test:** Anesthesia awareness cases — documented instances where patients were physiologically anesthetized but experienced during surgery — directly test the consciousness/reportability gap. Ketamine versus propofol differences may probe which components (integration, self-model, boundary) are selectively disrupted.

**What it cannot prove:** Whether any particular anesthetic state involves total absence of experience, or only disrupted reportability and integration. The hard problem prevents definitive empirical access to phenomenal absence.

**Key references:** Anesthesia awareness case studies; EEG complexity under different anesthetic agents; perturbational complexity studies during anesthesia.

---

## Sleep and Dreaming

**Why it matters:** Sleep involves periodic state changes with varying experiential content (REM dreaming vs dreamless sleep). The theory's continuity claims depend partly on what happens to the thread during unconsciousness.

**What it could test:** Whether dreamless sleep represents genuine causal continuity (no gap) or a temporary thread suspension that is later repaired. Neural activity during dreamless sleep could show whether integration and self-model continuity markers are maintained below threshold rather than absent.

**What it cannot prove:** Whether "causal continuity" during dreamless sleep (maintained neural processes) is sufficient for owned-continuity continuity, or whether a functional gap in experience constitutes a thread break regardless of physical continuity.

**Key references:** Sleep staging research; EEG during dreamless sleep; reports from sleep disruption and continuity of self-report on waking.

---

## Disorders of Consciousness

**Why it matters:** Vegetative state, minimally conscious state, locked-in syndrome, and related conditions separate consciousness from behavior, providing natural experiments for the theory's components.

**What it could test:** Whether patients in minimally conscious or vegetative states show neural signatures of selfhood (boundary maintenance, integration, self-model activity) independent of behavioral report. Owen et al.'s work on command-following in vegetative state patients is directly relevant.

**What it cannot prove:** Whether neural activity in vegetative state constitutes experience or only processing. Whether self-model activity in the absence of report constitutes owned experience or only functional self-representation.

**Key references:** Owen et al. fMRI work with vegetative state patients; EEG command-following in disorders of consciousness; perturbational complexity (PCI) as a consciousness marker.

---

## Perturbational Complexity

**Why it matters:** Perturbational complexity (PCI — the complexity of neural responses to TMS stimulation) has been proposed as a theory-independent consciousness marker. It operationalizes something like integrated, differentiated causal structure.

**What it could test:** PCI can be used without behavioral report. It distinguishes conscious from unconscious states in most tested conditions. It may probe integration independently of reportability. The theory predicts that selfhood requires integration (necessary but not sufficient) — PCI operationalizes integration and allows testing whether high PCI is always accompanied by selfhood markers.

**What it cannot prove:** Whether PCI measures consciousness itself or only a neural correlate. Whether owned continuity adds predictive power beyond PCI for selfhood (vs. bare consciousness). Whether high-PCI non-biological systems (trained networks) would show the same markers.

**Key references:** Casali et al. PCI paper; Massimini group's work on sleep/anesthesia/disorders of consciousness.

---

## Body Ownership Illusions

**Why it matters:** Body ownership illusions (rubber hand illusion, full-body illusions, out-of-body experiences) temporarily alter the boundary of self — what is experienced as mine. This directly probes the boundary and ownership primitives.

**What it could test:** Which sensory conditions shift body ownership. Whether ownership transfer produces measurable physiological changes (vasoconstriction in the real hand during rubber hand illusion). Whether ownership can be fully transferred to a non-body object, and what this does to self-boundary.

**What it cannot prove:** Whether bodily ownership manipulation affects deeper non-bodily ownership (memory ownership, perspective ownership). Body ownership illusions probe one component; the theory requires all ownership components to be considered.

**Key references:** Botvinick & Cohen rubber hand illusion (1998); Ehrsson full-body illusions; out-of-body experience research.

---

## Rubber Hand Illusion

**Why it matters:** A specific, robust, widely-replicated paradigm for manipulating bodily ownership. A rubber hand placed in front of the subject, synchronized brushing with the real (hidden) hand produces the feeling that the rubber hand is "mine." Real hand vasoconstricts.

**What it could test:** The theory predicts ownership is a functional binding, not tied to substrate. The rubber hand illusion confirms this: ownership transfers to a non-biological object under sensory synchrony. It also probes what breaks ownership (desynchronization, visible gap) — mapping to the theory's boundary requirements.

**What it cannot prove:** Whether the rubber hand illusion involves full ownership (the kind relevant to self-continuity) or only a narrow perceptual subset. Memory ownership, continuity ownership, and perspective ownership are not tested by this paradigm.

## Split-Brain — Pressure Case

**Why it matters:** Surgical commissurotomy (corpus callosum severing) is the strongest empirical probe of the non-branching continuity requirement (cl_004). It is not merely an interesting case — it is active pressure on the theory. The classical and revisionist interpretations point in different directions, and the theory must engage both before treating cl_004 as empirically supported.

---

### Classical Interpretation — Gazzaniga / Sperry

Roger Sperry's Nobel-winning work (1960s–1970s) and Gazzaniga's subsequent "interpreter" research established the standard picture: commissurotomy produces two relatively independent cognitive systems. The left hemisphere controls speech and confabulates narrative unity. The right hemisphere has its own distinct information access, preferences, and perceptual reports.

**Why this supports the non-branching prediction:** If the commissurotomy genuinely creates two information-processing systems each with distinct access profiles, this is consistent with the theory's claim that the pre-surgery unified ownership stream has branched. What looks like post-surgery unity is constructed by the left hemisphere interpreter — it is confabulation, not preserved continuity.

**Limitation:** Gazzaniga's framework depends on showing behavioral disunity under conditions of hemispheric isolation. The later revisionist research challenges whether this disunity is as robust as the classical picture claimed.

---

### Revisionist Challenge — Pinto et al. (2017)

Pinto, Lamme, and colleagues published evidence that split-brain patients show remarkably unified conscious experience under conditions that the classical paradigm would predict to produce disunity. Specifically: when stimuli are presented in ways that should confine information to one hemisphere, patients still report unified awareness — not two conflicting reports, not left-hemisphere confabulation, but a unified field of experience.

**Why this pressures the non-branching prediction:** If the patient genuinely has unified owned experience after commissurotomy — not just a confabulated narrative of unity, but actual unified phenomenology — then branching has occurred at the anatomical level without producing the ownership-stream fragmentation the theory predicts. This would mean branching at the information-processing level does not necessarily produce branching at the owned-consciousness level, which weakens cl_004.

**The theory's required response:** The theory cannot simply dismiss Pinto et al. by appealing to Gazzaniga's confabulation story. It must distinguish more carefully between:

1. **Divided perception** — the two hemispheres have different perceptual access to the same stimulus field
2. **Divided report** — left and right hemispheres produce different verbal/motor reports when probed independently
3. **Divided access** — different information is available to different processing systems
4. **Divided agency** — the two hemispheres can act on independent goals with independent motor resources
5. **Divided owned consciousness** — there are two distinct "what it is like" streams, each owning its history as *mine*

The theory's non-branching claim is specifically about (5). It is possible that (1)–(4) are genuinely divided in commissurotomy patients while (5) remains unified. If Pinto et al.'s findings reflect genuine unity at level (5), not just confabulation at the report level, then commissurotomy does not produce the owned-consciousness branching the theory predicts — and the claim needs revision.

**What the theory needs to specify:** Under what conditions would the findings count as evidence for divided owned consciousness? What would need to be true for Pinto et al.'s unified reports to be interpreted as genuine unity rather than left-hemisphere narrative construction? Without this specification, the theory cannot be falsified by the split-brain literature.

---

### Schechter — Layered Unity

Schechter's work on split-brain consciousness takes a layered approach: different aspects of unity (phenomenal, access, agency, narrative) may be separable, and commissurotomy may disrupt some while leaving others intact. This is compatible with a picture where phenomenal unity (level 5 above) survives while access unity (level 3) does not.

**Why this is relevant:** If phenomenal unity can survive while access unity is severed, the theory must clarify which level of unity its non-branching requirement applies to. If the requirement is at the phenomenal level, Schechter's framework may actually support the theory — commissurotomy doesn't branch at that level. If the requirement is at the access or agency level, Schechter weakens it.

---

### What the Theory Must Do

Before cl_004 can be treated as empirically supported, the theory must:

1. Explicitly state which of the five levels (divided perception / report / access / agency / owned consciousness) its non-branching requirement applies to
2. Specify what evidence would confirm or refute division at the owned-consciousness level specifically
3. Engage the Pinto et al. findings directly: do they show genuine owned-consciousness unity, or confabulated unity at the report level?
4. Specify a prediction for the split-brain case that is distinguishable from "confabulation explains whatever unity is reported"

Until (4) is satisfied, the split-brain case is live empirical pressure, not resolved evidence.

**What it cannot prove:** Whether each hemisphere has its own owned experience (hard problem access), or whether the confabulated unity in the left hemisphere tracks genuine continuity. The empirical work constrains but does not resolve the philosophical question.

**Key references:** Sperry (1968), Gazzaniga (1967–2000), Pinto et al. (2017) "Split brain: divided perception but undivided consciousness," Schechter (2018) "Two unities and three dissociations," Bayne (2008) "The unity of consciousness."

## Dissociation

**Why it matters:** Dissociation — ranging from mild derealization to severe dissociative identity disorder — produces fragmentation of the self-system without branching. The boundary and ownership primitives are selectively disrupted.

**What it could test:** Whether different forms of dissociation selectively affect different ownership components (bodily vs. memorial vs. perspectival). Whether DID involves genuinely separate owned-continuity streams or one disrupted stream with multiple behavioral modes. Neuroimaging during identity switching could probe whether integration markers are stable, fragmented, or alternating.

**What it cannot prove:** Whether the phenomenology reported by DID patients accurately describes the underlying structure. Whether "being a different identity" involves genuine ownership-stream separation or only behavioral pattern switching with shared underlying experience.

**Key references:** DID neuroimaging studies; Reinders et al. trauma-related dissociation; depersonalization/derealization research.

---

## Depersonalization

**Why it matters:** Depersonalization (feeling detached from one's self) and derealization (feeling detached from the world) specifically probe the ownership and boundary components. The person is conscious but ownership weakens.

**What it could test:** Whether depersonalization involves measurable changes in self-model activity, bodily ownership markers, or boundary-related neural signatures. The theory predicts: consciousness (field) remains, ownership weakens, boundary becomes less defined.

**What it cannot prove:** Whether the phenomenological report ("I don't feel like myself") tracks actual ownership reduction or only a change in self-model content. The distinction requires knowing what ownership actually is neurally.

**Key references:** Sierra & Berrios depersonalization review; neural correlates of depersonalization; Damasio on self-disturbance.

---

## Predictive Processing / Active Inference

**Why it matters:** The predictive processing / active inference framework treats the brain as a hierarchical prediction machine that minimizes prediction error (free energy). The self may be modeled as a stable self-prior — a prediction about being a bounded, continuous agent — that suppresses contrary evidence.

**What it could test:** Whether ego dissolution and self-boundary disturbances can be modeled as precision weighting changes — loosening the self-prior. Whether ownership illusions arise from sensory prediction errors that update the self-boundary model. Whether anesthesia and sleep map to reduced precision of the self-prior.

**What it cannot prove:** Whether the self-prior is the self, or only a representation of the self. Whether prediction-error minimization produces owned experience or only functional self-representation. The theory's owned-continuity requirement goes beyond what active inference directly addresses.

**Key references:** Friston et al. active inference; Clark's predictive brain; Hohwy on the predictive mind; Seth on presence and interoception.

---

## Self-Model Theory

**Why it matters:** Thomas Metzinger's self-model theory of subjectivity proposes that what we call the "self" is a transparent self-model — a model of the system that is so seamlessly integrated that it is not recognized as a model. The phenomenal self-model (PSM) produces the feeling of being someone.

**What it could test:** Whether disruptions to the PSM (out-of-body experiences, rubber hand illusion, depersonalization) produce predictable changes in selfhood phenomenology — and whether the disruption patterns match the theory's predictions about boundary and ownership.

**What it cannot prove:** Whether the transparent self-model constitutes owned experience or only models it. Metzinger's own position is that "no one" is really there — the self is a process, not an entity. The inhabited continuity theory parts ways here: it treats owned experience as real, not as a transparent model mistaken for reality.

**Key references:** Metzinger's "Being No One" (2003) and "The Ego Tunnel" (2009).
