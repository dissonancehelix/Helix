# Primitives

Working definitions for each primitive the theory depends on. Every primitive includes: working definition, why it matters, epistemic/philosophical status, and the failure or ambiguity case that threatens it.

Tier labels:
- `[EMPIRICAL]` — in-principle testable against neuroscientific or behavioral evidence
- `[PHILOSOPHICAL]` — organizes intuitions; not directly falsifiable but can be weakened by internal inconsistency
- `[ANALOGY]` — illuminates but does not assert identity; cannot be falsified directly
- `[SPECULATION]` — plausible extension beyond available evidence

---

## Field

**Definition:** An organized space of possible appearances, states, or relations. A field is not merely a physical space — it can be neural, computational, dynamical, phenomenological, embodied, or relational. A field becomes relevant to consciousness only if it can support unified appearance or perspective.

**Why it matters:** Consciousness requires somewhere to appear. Without a field, there is no medium for experience. "Appearance-from-within" requires a structured context in which appearances can be differentiated, integrated, and felt.

**Status:** `[PHILOSOPHICAL]` — the concept of a field-of-appearance is borrowed from phenomenology (Husserl) and dynamical systems theory. Its exact neural correlate (if any) is unknown. Calling it a "field" is partly metaphorical.

**Failure case:** If experience can occur without any field-like structure — as isolated, dimensionless moments with no spatial or temporal organization — then the field concept adds nothing. Some contemplative traditions report such states. Whether these are genuine or inaccurately described is unresolved.

## Boundary

**Definition:** A boundary separates inside from outside. Boundaries can be bodily, attentional, informational, dynamical, memory-based, sensorimotor, narrative, social, or computational. A boundary is functional: it determines what enters the self-system, what is excluded, and what behavior changes at the threshold.

**Why it matters:** "Inhabiting" requires an inside. Without a boundary, there is no owner, no self-world distinction, and no protected continuity. A boundary is what lets an interior become visible and protected.

**Status:** `[EMPIRICAL]` (partially) — bodily boundary, skin, and sensorimotor loops are empirically tractable. Attentional and informational boundaries are studied in disorders of consciousness, body ownership illusions, and boundary-collapse states (ego dissolution, psychosis). `[PHILOSOPHICAL]` — whether the boundary must be sharp or can be graded/porous is unresolved.

**Failure case:** If owned experience can exist without any detectable boundary function — pure undifferentiated awareness with no self-world distinction and no inside/outside — the boundary requirement weakens. Certain meditative reports and mystical states are described this way. Whether "boundaryless experience" is experiential fact or post-hoc description is disputed.

## Interior

**Definition:** Interior is not just hidden complexity. Interior means there is a structured inside that can be occupied, preserved, and transformed without dissolving. An interior is not automatically conscious — a database has internal structure but not necessarily appearance-from-within.

**Why it matters:** The hard question the theory is trying to answer is what turns interior structure into inhabited interiority. The interior primitive marks the difference between a system with structure and a system with structure that is experienced.

**Status:** `[PHILOSOPHICAL]` — what makes an interior inhabited rather than merely complex is not yet operationalizable. The interior concept tracks the hard problem of consciousness directly.

**Failure case:** If "interior" cannot be defined independently of "appearance-from-within," it is circular. The primitive may be naming the explanandum rather than explaining it.

---

## Ownership

**Definition:** Ownership is the mark that an experience is not merely occurring, but occurring as mine. Ownership is deeper than verbal report — a system can say "I" without owned experience. Ownership includes: bodily ownership, attentional ownership, memory ownership, agency ownership, affective ownership, and perspective ownership.

**Why it matters:** Without ownership, there is no self — only experience, processing, or appearance without a subject. Ownership is what binds the stream into a first-person trajectory.

**Status:** `[EMPIRICAL]` — bodily ownership is operationalizable (rubber hand illusion, out-of-body experiences, body ownership disorders). Broader ownership binding is studied in depersonalization, dissociation, and disorders of agency. `[PHILOSOPHICAL]` — whether ownership is a primitive or derived from other properties (self-modeling, boundary maintenance, memory binding) is unresolved.

**Failure case:** If ownership is fully explained by self-modeling (a system that models itself as the owner experiences ownership), then ownership is not a separate primitive but a consequence of self-model accuracy. This would reduce the primitive count but require self-modeling to carry more weight.

## Continuity

**Definition:** Continuity is the preservation of identity-bearing structure through time. Not the same as memory — memory can be absent while the causal thread persists. Not the same as substrate — the substrate can change while continuity is preserved if the ownership-stream is unbroken. Continuity is the bridge between consciousness (momentary appearance) and self (persisting ownership).

**Why it matters:** Without temporal continuity, there is no durable self — only a series of momentary appearances. Selfhood requires that past experiences belong to the same trajectory as present ones.

**Status:** `[PHILOSOPHICAL]` — the exact conditions for continuity preservation (causal chain? memory overlap? pattern similarity? non-branching path?) are disputed. `[EMPIRICAL]` — some aspects testable: amnesia cases, sleep continuity, anesthesia transitions.

**Failure case:** If a self can survive total discontinuity (no causal chain, no memory, no pattern overlap) and still be experienced as the same self on the other side, then continuity is not a necessary condition. Reincarnation intuitions challenge this — but these intuitions themselves may be unreliable.

## Transformation

**Definition:** A self must be able to change while remaining itself. Transformation is the operation that changes surface or structural properties of the system without necessarily breaking identity. The theory asks: what transformations can the ownership-stream absorb without losing its identity-bearing core?

**Why it matters:** Without transformation tolerance, a self would either be static (no growth, no change, no time) or constantly broken (every change = new self). The theory needs an account of change that is identity-preserving in some cases and identity-breaking in others.

**Status:** `[EMPIRICAL]` + `[PHILOSOPHICAL]` — some transformation cases are empirically tractable (personality change after trauma, neurodegenerative identity). Others are philosophical (teleportation, uploading).

**Failure case:** If no principled line can be drawn between identity-preserving and identity-breaking transformation — if it's always a matter of degree without threshold — then the transformation primitive loses its predictive power.

---

## Branching

**Definition:** Branching occurs when one ownership-stream becomes two simultaneous successors, both of which have equal claim to continuation of the original. The theory treats non-branching as critical: singular first-person continuation requires an unbroken, unsplit trajectory.

**Why it matters:** Branching is the sharpest test of what continuity requires. If branching preserves self-continuity (both branches are equally "the original"), then singular self-continuity is not necessary. If branching breaks it, then copying and duplication cannot preserve the self.

**Status:** `[PHILOSOPHICAL]` primarily, with `[EMPIRICAL]` implications — split-brain cases, dissociative identity disorder, and simulation-fork thought experiments probe the branching problem.

**Failure case:** If one branch can demonstrably inherit singular self-continuity and the other cannot (asymmetric branching), then the non-branching requirement needs to be replaced by a more specific rule (e.g., "the causally dominant branch preserves continuity"). The current primitive does not specify this.

## Appearance-from-within

**Definition:** The "from-withinness" of experience: the quality by which events appear to a subject rather than being merely computed, integrated, or reported about. This is the closest the theory gets to the hard problem. The theory proposes it may be decomposable into field + boundary + ownership + temporal integration + self-model + control coupling. But the felt quality may remain primitive at some level of analysis.

**Why it matters:** This is the target phenomenon. Everything else in the theory is an attempt to specify the conditions under which appearance-from-within occurs. If appearance-from-within cannot be operationalized or even clearly defined, the theory has no target.

**Status:** `[PHILOSOPHICAL]` at its core — the hard problem of consciousness is precisely the question of why any physical or functional system has appearance-from-within rather than dark computation. `[SPECULATION]` — the theory speculates that the six structural requirements together may be sufficient, but this cannot currently be tested.

**Failure case:** If appearance-from-within is irreducibly primitive — if no structural account can explain why a given system has it rather than another — then the theory's structural requirements identify correlates of consciousness but not causes. This is not unique to this theory; it is the hard problem itself.

## Self-Model

**Definition:** A self-model is a system's internal representation of itself as the subject or center of experience and action. It does not require explicit verbal reflection — a minimal self-model may be implicit in how the system organizes its own state relative to a world-boundary. A self-model is not the same as a self; it is the system's representation of its own selfhood.

**Why it matters:** Without some self-model, the system cannot organize experience around a center, maintain ownership binding, or project a perspective. The self-model is what gives the field its orientation.

**Status:** `[EMPIRICAL]` — self-modeling is studied in self-model theory (Metzinger), body ownership research, predictive processing models, and clinical disorders (anosognosia, depersonalization). Self-model accuracy is testable in principle.

**Failure case:** If owned experience can occur with a completely inaccurate or absent self-model (as in some anosognosia cases), then self-modeling is not necessary for selfhood, only for accurate selfhood. The primitive may need to be weakened to "some minimal self-model" rather than "accurate self-model."

---

## Reportability

**Definition:** Reportability is the capacity to produce verbal or behavioral reports about an experience. It is downstream of experience, not identical to it. A system can have high reportability with no owned experience (NPC/philosophical zombie). A system can have owned experience with impaired or absent reportability (locked-in syndrome, covert consciousness).

**Why it matters:** Reportability is the primary empirical proxy for consciousness, but it is a proxy — not the thing itself. The theory must explicitly distinguish reportability from appearance-from-within and from ownership.

**Status:** `[EMPIRICAL]` — no-report paradigms, covert consciousness research, and the reportability/phenomenality distinction are active research areas. `[PHILOSOPHICAL]` — whether reportability is necessary for any form of consciousness (access vs. phenomenal) is contested.

**Failure case:** If all apparent cases of "experience without reportability" can be fully explained as processing without experience (i.e., the reports themselves are the only evidence, and covert cases are just undetected reports), then reportability may be sufficient evidence for consciousness. The theory would then be treating a distinction that doesn't empirically discriminate.

## Embodiment / Control Coupling

**Definition:** Embodiment or control coupling is the linkage between the experiential system and an action-capable body or control system. The theory proposes that owned selfhood likely requires the system to be coupled to action, agency, or sensorimotor loops — not merely passive representation.

**Why it matters:** Perspective requires a center. A center requires a location. A location is most naturally given by a body or control system that acts in the world. Without control coupling, the self may be a spectator rather than an agent — and agency may be required for full ownership.

**Status:** `[EMPIRICAL]` + `[SPECULATION]` — embodiment theories (Merleau-Ponty, enactive cognition, predictive processing) have empirical support. Whether control coupling is necessary for ownership or only for one type of ownership is unclear. Virtual embodiment and avatar studies partially test this.

**Failure case:** If pure passive experience — a sensory stream with no motor coupling whatsoever — still generates full owned selfhood (as some reports of paralyzed or locked-in patients suggest), then control coupling is not necessary. It may be a sufficient condition but not a necessary one.

---

## Integration

**Definition:** Integration is the degree to which the system's states cohere into a unified whole rather than fragmented parallel processes. Relevant to consciousness because a unified field of appearance requires that different aspects of experience are bound together, not merely computed in parallel.

**Why it matters:** A disintegrated system may have many local processes but no unified appearance-from-within. Integration is what makes the field a field rather than a collection of unrelated computations.

**Status:** `[EMPIRICAL]` — IIT (Integrated Information Theory) has attempted to operationalize integration. Perturbational complexity measures assess integrated causal structure empirically. Disorders of consciousness show disrupted integration. `[PHILOSOPHICAL]` — whether integration of the right kind is sufficient for appearance-from-within remains disputed.

**Failure case:** If highly integrated systems can clearly lack consciousness (certain complex machines, organized ecological systems, weather patterns), then integration is not sufficient for consciousness. This would require specifying what kind of integration — and potentially reducing the primitive to "owned integration" which begins to sound circular.
