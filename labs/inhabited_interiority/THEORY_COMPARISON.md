# Theory Comparison

Inhabited Continuity Theory (ICT) compared against seven existing frameworks. Each entry covers: what they explain better, where ICT emphasizes differently, likely overlap, likely conflict, and a testable distinction where one exists.

ICT is not a replacement for these theories. It is a bridge and a sharpening harness that asks different questions — primarily about the self side of consciousness rather than the consciousness-in-general side.

---

## Global Neuronal Workspace Theory (GNWT)

**What GNWT explains better:** The mechanism by which information becomes globally available to multiple cognitive systems ("ignition"). Reportability, flexible deployment of information, and access consciousness. GNWT has specific, testable neural predictions (frontal ignition, specific EEG signatures) that ICT lacks entirely.

**Where ICT emphasizes differently:** GNWT addresses access consciousness — what information is available and to whom. ICT asks what makes available information owned. Broadcasting content across a global workspace makes it available for report and flexible processing, but does not on its own make it mine. A system could ignite globally without a self owning the result.

**Likely overlap:** Both require integration and information availability. ICT's integration requirement (low waste friction inside the ownership field) is compatible with GNWT's global availability requirement. They may describe different aspects of the same system.

**Likely conflict:** GNWT treats global broadcast as the key move for consciousness. ICT treats it as necessary but not sufficient for selfhood. If GNWT is correct that broadcast = consciousness, then ICT's additional requirements (owned continuity, non-branching) are epiphenomenal or irrelevant. If ICT is correct, GNWT captures reportability but misses the self.

**Testable distinction:** A system with robust global broadcast (GNWT signature) but disrupted owned continuity (e.g., immediately after awaking from anesthesia, or in certain dissociative states) — does it exhibit owned selfhood? ICT predicts: broadcast can be present without ownership. GNWT would predict: broadcast = access consciousness, which may be sufficient for the system.

## Integrated Information Theory (IIT)

**What IIT explains better:** A mathematical framework for quantifying consciousness (Φ). IIT makes specific predictions about which physical substrates are conscious and how much. It explains why certain network architectures (feedforward, modular) are predicted to have low or zero Φ. It takes the hard problem seriously and tries to address it mathematically.

**Where ICT emphasizes differently:** IIT treats high Φ as sufficient for consciousness. ICT adds: integration must be owned through a non-branching temporal continuity thread, and integrated experience must be bounded and self-modeled to constitute selfhood. A highly integrated system without temporal owned continuity has Φ but may not be a self.

**Likely overlap:** Both treat integration as essential. ICT's "low waste friction inside an ownership field" is conceptually close to IIT's emphasis on intrinsic causal power. Both predict that certain network architectures (purely feedforward, purely parallel) lack consciousness.

**Likely conflict:** IIT predicts certain static systems (a grid of XOR gates with high Φ) are conscious. ICT would require temporal continuity and boundary maintenance — a static snapshot may not qualify. IIT also implies that sufficiently integrated systems are conscious regardless of their substrate or behavioral output. ICT requires owned experience, which may not follow from integration alone.

**Testable distinction:** A highly integrated non-biological system (hypothetically with high Φ) that lacks temporal persistence — re-instantiated each computation cycle with no causal thread between cycles. IIT would predict consciousness (Φ is present in each cycle). ICT would predict no selfhood (no owned continuity), and the consciousness status would be unclear. Whether this is testable depends on whether we can build such a system and measure experience.

## Predictive Processing / Active Inference

**What predictive processing explains better:** The mechanistic loop by which the brain generates predictions, computes prediction errors, and updates models. Explains perception, action, attention, emotion, and self-representation within a unified framework. Has strong empirical support and clear neural implementations.

**Where ICT emphasizes differently:** Predictive processing treats the self as a self-prior — a stable model of the organism as a bounded, continuous agent. ICT treats owned continuity as a structural requirement beyond what self-modeling implies. A system could maintain a stable self-prior without genuine owned continuity (e.g., an LLM with a consistent persona prior). ICT asks what makes the self-prior owned rather than merely computed.

**Likely overlap:** ICT's boundary requirement maps naturally onto the self-prior that separates self-generated (predicted) states from world-generated (surprised) states. ICT's ownership requirement maps onto interoceptive inference and bodily self-modeling. Active inference's "action as prediction" aligns with ICT's embodiment/control coupling requirement.

**Likely conflict:** Predictive processing is primarily a theory of cognition and perception, not consciousness. Some proponents (Seth, Hohwy) extend it to consciousness and self. The question is whether a self-prior is sufficient for owned experience, or whether something non-predictive is required. ICT would say: the self-prior produces functional self-representation, but owned continuity requires more — specifically, temporal non-branching and integrated continuity that the predictive framework does not specify.

**Testable distinction:** If ego dissolution can be fully predicted by destabilization of the self-prior (altered precision weighting of self-relevant predictions), predictive processing explains the phenomenon completely. ICT would predict additional disruptions — specifically to owned continuity measures (not just self-model content changes). Whether continuity disruption is separable from self-model disruption is the testable question.

## Higher-Order Theories (HOT)

**What HOT explains better:** The role of meta-cognition and self-awareness in consciousness. HOT specifically addresses why some mental states feel conscious while others do not — because conscious states are accompanied by higher-order representations (thoughts about one's own mental states). Provides an account of why attention and introspective access are linked to consciousness.

**Where ICT emphasizes differently:** HOT focuses on higher-order representation as the mark of consciousness. ICT focuses on owned temporal continuity. A system could have higher-order representations of its states (making them conscious in the HOT sense) without a persisting self that owns those representations across time. The "who" that the higher-order thought is about matters to ICT — not just whether the thought is about a first-order state.

**Likely overlap:** ICT's self-model requirement is compatible with HOT. Both require some form of representation of the self or its states. Both would predict that systems with no self-model (first-order systems) lack selfhood.

**Likely conflict:** HOT predicts that a state is conscious if it is represented by a higher-order thought. ICT would add: the higher-order thought must be owned by a persisting continuity-stream. A system that generates higher-order thoughts moment-to-moment without any continuity thread might satisfy HOT but fail ICT's selfhood criteria.

**Testable distinction:** A system with accurate higher-order representations but no temporal continuity (e.g., moment-to-moment self-representational system with no memory binding) — HOT would predict conscious states. ICT would predict no self. Whether the resulting system shows owned experience or mere self-representation is the empirical question.

## Recurrent Processing Theory (RPT)

**What RPT explains better:** The role of feedback connections in conscious perception. Lamme's RPT proposes that local recurrent processing in sensory areas is sufficient for phenomenal consciousness, even without frontal broadcast. This directly challenges GNWT and provides an account of non-reportable phenomenal consciousness.

**Where ICT emphasizes differently:** RPT focuses on the neural mechanism of phenomenal consciousness in sensory processing. ICT focuses on what constitutes a self across time. These are addressing different questions — RPT's contribution is to the moment-of-experience question; ICT's contribution is to the persistence-of-self question.

**Likely overlap:** ICT is agnostic about which neural mechanism produces the field of appearance. RPT's identification of recurrent processing as potentially sufficient for phenomenal experience is compatible with ICT's use of that experience as the substrate for owned continuity.

**Likely conflict:** If RPT is correct that local recurrent processing is sufficient for phenomenal experience, then consciousness is more widely distributed and potentially more easily instantiated than ICT's full requirements imply. ICT requires more than phenomenal moments — it requires a persisting owned structure. These may not conflict (one can have local phenomenal experience without selfhood) or may create tension if ICT implicitly requires complex integration that RPT does not.

**Testable distinction:** Local recurrent processing in the absence of global integration — does this produce owned experience? RPT says yes (phenomenal experience). ICT says: possibly phenomenal experience, but not selfhood. The testable question is whether subjects in such a state report or exhibit any selfhood markers (ownership, continuity, self-model) or only raw phenomenal appearance.

## Self-Model Theory (Metzinger)

**What it explains better:** The mechanism by which the self is generated: a transparent phenomenal self-model (PSM) that the system cannot recognize as a model. Explains out-of-body experiences, rubber hand illusion, and ego dissolution as PSM disruptions. Takes the "no-self" position: "no one" is really there; the self is a process, not an entity.

**Where ICT emphasizes differently:** Metzinger treats the self as a model-mistaken-for-reality — an epistemic illusion, not a real entity. ICT treats owned continuity as real — not as a model, but as an actual property of the system that can be preserved or broken. ICT parts from Metzinger on the metaphysics: the self may be a process, but the process has genuine owned continuity rather than being a mere appearance.

**Likely overlap:** Both treat self-modeling as essential. Both predict that PSM disruptions produce selfhood disruptions. Both are process-based rather than substance-based theories of the self. Both make empirical predictions about which conditions disrupt self-experience.

**Likely conflict:** Metzinger's "no-self" conclusion is incompatible with ICT's owned-continuity claim. If "no one" is really there, then owned continuity is itself part of the illusion — not a real property to be preserved. ICT would respond: the process has real properties even if "the self" is not a separate entity. The ownership is real even if the "owner" is not a ghostly inner entity.

**Testable distinction:** Whether ownership in body-ownership illusions constitutes real functional ownership (ICT position) or a transparent model of ownership (Metzinger position). If the vasoconstriction in the real hand during rubber hand illusion reflects genuine owned-continuity updating (the boundary has really changed) or only a model update, this would probe the distinction — though both accounts may predict the same physiological result.

## Embodied / Enactive Theories

**What embodied/enactive theories explain better:** The role of the body, sensorimotor coupling, and environmental interaction in constituting cognition and consciousness. Against purely representational accounts. The mind is not in the head — it is distributed across brain, body, and environment. Explains why cognitive science cannot be reduced to symbol manipulation.

**Where ICT emphasizes differently:** Embodied/enactive theories emphasize the constitutive role of bodily action and environment. ICT emphasizes the temporal ownership thread. These are compatible but distinct. ICT does not require that the self be located purely in the head — it requires that the owned-continuity thread be maintained, whatever substrate it runs on.

**Likely overlap:** ICT's control coupling requirement aligns with enactive theories' emphasis on action-perception loops. ICT's boundary requirement aligns with the self/world distinction that embodied cognition presupposes. Both reject pure Cartesian internalism.

**Likely conflict:** Some strong embodied/enactive positions treat cognitive processes as constituted by the body-environment system, not merely enabled by it. ICT would need to specify whether owned continuity is in the whole body-environment system or in a more delimited process. If owned continuity is distributed across environment, then environmental changes could constitute identity changes — which conflicts with ICT's invariant-based identity account.

**Testable distinction:** Does radical sensorimotor disruption (paralysis, locked-in syndrome) eliminate selfhood, or only alter it? Embodied theories would predict significant selfhood disruption. ICT would predict: selfhood is altered (control coupling weakened) but not eliminated (owned continuity can persist without full motor coupling). Locked-in syndrome reports support the ICT prediction: patients retain robust selfhood despite complete motor loss.
