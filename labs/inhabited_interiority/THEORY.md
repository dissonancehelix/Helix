# Inhabited Continuity Theory
## Working Draft v0.1 — Single-Document Summary

This document preserves the current state of the theory so it does not get lost during workspace refactors.

The theory is provisional. It is not presented as proven neuroscience, metaphysics, religious belief, or a finished theory of consciousness. It is a structured research program: a set of primitives, hypotheses, falsifiers, and test paths for asking what makes a structured field become inhabited from within, and what must be preserved for that inhabited interior to remain the same self through time.

---

## 1. Core Question

The central question is:

> What makes a structured field become inhabited from within, and what must be preserved for that inhabited interior to remain the same self through time?

A sharper version:

> Under what conditions does a structured process become not merely organized, reactive, or intelligent, but experientially owned from the inside?

And the identity version:

> How much can a system transform before the inhabited ownership-stream is no longer preserved?

`domains/worldview/WORLDVIEW.md` owns the operator's religious/spiritual orientation, politics, morals, institutional trust, and metaphysical leaning. This file owns the research question of consciousness, selfhood, continuity, branching, and appearance-from-within.

The theory is trying to distinguish:

- structure from experience
- cognition from consciousness
- continuity from memory alone
- self from consciousness
- transformation from replacement
- identity-preserving change from branching
- reportability from appearance-from-within

## 3. Core Intuition

A system may be highly structured without being inhabited.

A system may compute, respond, model, report, adapt, or integrate information without there being anything it is like from within.

The theory proposes that consciousness and selfhood require more than information processing. They require a structured field with:

- boundary
- temporal continuity
- internal integration
- transformation tolerance
- ownership
- self-modeling
- non-branching continuity
- embodied or control-linked perspective
- preservation of an interior stream through time

The key intuition:

> Consciousness is the field in which self can appear; self is the stabilized ownership-structure that inhabits that field through time.

So consciousness and self are not identical.

Consciousness is closer to the field of appearance.
Self is closer to the continuity-bearing occupant or ownership-pattern within that field.

## 4. Consciousness vs Self

### Consciousness

Consciousness is the appearance-field: the space in which experience is present at all.

It includes:

- subjective appearance
- phenomenal presence
- felt immediacy
- world-disclosure
- the capacity for “something it is like”

Consciousness may exist without a strong autobiographical self, as in certain altered states, minimal experience, meditation reports, infant consciousness, dream states, or fragmented pathological states.

### Self

Self is the stabilized ownership-pattern within consciousness.

It includes:

- this experience is happening to me
- this body or control-center is mine
- this stream has continuity
- this transformation is still mine
- this memory belongs to the same lived trajectory
- this perspective is centered

Self is not merely a narrative. It is also not merely memory. It is a continuity-preserving, boundary-maintaining, perspective-organizing structure.

### Relationship

The proposed relationship:

> Consciousness is the field of appearance.
> Self is the continuity-bearing ownership structure that inhabits the field.

Or shorter:

> Consciousness is appearing. Self is owned continuity.

## 5. The Central Primitives

The theory currently depends on these primitives.

### 5.1 Field

A field is an organized space of possible appearances, states, or relations.

In this theory, a field is not merely a physical space. It can be neural, computational, dynamical, phenomenological, embodied, or relational.

A field becomes relevant to consciousness only if it can support unified appearance or perspective.

### 5.2 Boundary

A boundary separates inside from outside.

Without a boundary, there is no owner, no interior, no self-world distinction, no protected continuity.

Boundaries can be:

- bodily
- attentional
- informational
- dynamical
- memory-based
- sensorimotor
- narrative
- social
- computational

The theory treats boundary as essential because “inhabiting” requires an inside.

### 5.3 Interior

Interior is not just hidden complexity.

Interior means there is a structured inside that can be occupied, preserved, and transformed without dissolving.

An interior is not automatically conscious. A database has structure, but not necessarily appearance-from-within.

The hard question is what turns interior structure into inhabited interiority.

### 5.4 Ownership

Ownership is the mark that an experience is not merely occurring, but occurring as mine.

Ownership is deeper than verbal report. A system can say “I” without owned experience.

Ownership includes:

- bodily ownership
- attentional ownership
- memory ownership
- agency ownership
- affective ownership
- perspective ownership

### 5.5 Continuity

Continuity is the preservation of identity-bearing structure through time.

No time means no before/after, no memory, no transformation, no persistence, no self-continuity.

Time is not optional. If time is frozen, there is no context in which a self can remain itself.

Continuity is the bridge between consciousness and self.

### 5.6 Transformation

A self must be able to change while remaining itself.

The theory asks:

> What transformations can the ownership-stream absorb without breaking identity?

This connects to:

- Ship of Theseus
- teleportation
- memory replacement
- brain uploading
- personality change
- trauma
- sleep and waking
- split-brain cases
- dissociation
- simulation branching
- reincarnation concepts
- narrative continuity
- character continuity across fiction

### 5.7 Branching

Branching is a special threat to self-continuity.

If one stream becomes two, both may claim continuity, but the original first-person ownership may not be singularly preserved.

The theory currently treats non-branching as critical:

> An ownership-stream can transform, but if it branches into multiple equally valid successors, singular self-continuity is no longer preserved in the ordinary sense.

### 5.8 Appearance-from-within

This is the hardest primitive.

The theory asks whether appearance-from-within can be decomposed further, or whether it is primitive.

Current position:

- It may be decomposable into field + boundary + ownership + temporal integration + self-model + control coupling.
- But the felt “from-withinness” may remain the point where conceptual sharpening stops and empirical science must begin.

## 6. Current Thesis

The working thesis:

> A conscious self is an inhabited ownership-stream: a temporally continuous, bounded, internally integrated field of appearance that preserves ownership through transformation without branching.

More formal version:

> Selfhood emerges when a conscious field maintains a stable ownership model across time, integrates transformations into a single continuity-stream, and preserves an inside/outside boundary sufficient for experiences, memories, body-states, and actions to be owned as belonging to the same subject.

Even more compressed:

> Self = owned continuity inside a conscious field.

The theory is not claiming that every conscious state requires a full self. It is claiming that selfhood requires consciousness plus ownership-continuity.

## 7. Structural Requirements for Inhabited Selfhood

A system is a stronger candidate for inhabited selfhood if it has the following.

### 7.1 Temporal Integration

The system must integrate past, present, and anticipated future.

Without temporal integration, there is no durable ownership.

### 7.2 Boundary Maintenance

The system must distinguish self-relevant from non-self-relevant states.

A self cannot exist if every process is equally inside.

### 7.3 Ownership Binding

The system must bind experiences, actions, body-states, and memories into a first-person continuity.

Ownership binding is not the same as storage.

### 7.4 Transformation Absorption

The system must absorb change without losing its identity-bearing structure.

A self must be dynamic, not static.

### 7.5 Non-Branching Stream

The system must preserve a singular ownership trajectory.

Branching creates multiple successors, which may preserve information but not singular first-person continuation.

### 7.6 Recursive Self-Modeling

The system likely needs some model of itself as the subject or center of experience/action.

This does not require explicit verbal self-reflection, but it does require self-world organization.

### 7.7 Control Coupling

The system must likely be coupled to action, agency, or embodied control.

Pure passive representation may be insufficient for owned selfhood.

### 7.8 Low-Waste Internal Integration

The system must not merely contain many processes. Those processes must cohere enough to maintain an ownership-stream.

This is where the newer “structural friction minimization” idea enters the theory.

## 8. Structural Friction and Consciousness

A major bridge from the personal ontology into the consciousness theory is the distinction between useful friction and waste friction.

Useful friction:

- reveals structure
- generates adaptation
- creates learning
- enables differentiation
- supports stable transformation

Waste friction:

- creates incoherence
- disrupts integration
- fragments ownership
- burns energy without increasing structure
- creates noise without self-correction

The consciousness-relevant hypothesis:

> A self requires not the absence of friction, but the reduction of waste friction inside an ownership-bearing field.

This does not mean a conscious system must be smooth or simple. It may require pressure, conflict, error, and correction.

But if internal processes cannot be integrated into a coherent ownership-stream, the self fragments or fails to stabilize.

Possible application:

- ordinary selfhood = stable integration
- dissociation = disrupted ownership binding
- split-brain = altered or divided ownership integration
- dreams = changed boundary/control coupling
- anesthesia = collapse of integrated appearance
- psychosis = altered self-world boundary and prediction structure
- ego dissolution = weakened self-model / ownership center
- flow states = low waste friction + high useful constraint

## 9. Relation to Existing Theories

This theory is not meant to replace existing consciousness theories yet. It is a bridge/harness that asks different questions.

### 9.1 Global Neuronal Workspace Theory

GNWT emphasizes global availability or broadcasting of information across cognitive systems.

This theory asks:

- Is global availability enough for appearance-from-within?
- Does broadcasting create reportability but not necessarily ownership?
- What must be added for globally available content to be mine?

### 9.2 Integrated Information Theory

IIT emphasizes integrated causal structure.

This theory asks:

- Is integration enough?
- What kind of integration supports owned continuity?
- Can a highly integrated system lack a self?
- Does integration need boundary and temporal ownership constraints?

### 9.3 Predictive Processing / Active Inference

Predictive processing and active inference emphasize modeling hidden causes, reducing prediction error/surprise, and action-perception loops.

This theory connects strongly here because selfhood may require a system that models itself as a bounded cause within a world.

Questions:

- Is selfhood a stabilized self-model used to minimize uncertainty?
- Does ownership arise when predictions are centered around a body/control system?
- Can ego dissolution be modeled as altered precision or destabilized self-modeling?

### 9.4 Self-Model Theory

Self-model theories treat the self as an ongoing model/process rather than a metaphysical object.

This theory agrees that the self may be process-like, but adds emphasis on:

- inhabitedness
- continuity preservation
- transformation tolerance
- non-branching ownership-streams

### 9.5 No-Report Paradigms

No-report paradigms matter because reportability may not be consciousness itself.

This theory must avoid confusing:

- what a system can report
- what a system can access
- what appears from within
- what is owned by a self

### 9.6 Perturbational Complexity

Perturbational complexity approaches are relevant because they test whether a system can sustain differentiated/integrated causal responses.

This theory may use similar logic conceptually:

> If a field cannot respond in a differentiated yet integrated way, it is a weaker candidate for consciousness.

But the theory adds:

> If that integration is not owned through time, it is a weaker candidate for selfhood.

## 11. Portable Self-Continuity Carrier

A major sub-question:

> Is there any possible portable carrier of self-continuity?

Candidate carriers:

- same biological organism
- same brain process
- same memory structure
- same self-model
- same causal chain
- same body-world control loop
- same information pattern
- same non-branching transformation path
- same ownership-stream

The theory currently leans toward:

> Information pattern alone is probably not enough if it can be copied and branched.

A stronger continuity carrier would need:

- causal continuity
- temporal continuity
- ownership preservation
- non-branching transition
- transformation absorption
- stable self-model relation
- perhaps embodied/control continuity

This is where teleportation and uploading become hard.

If teleportation destroys one body and creates one perfect successor, the question is whether the ownership-stream transformed or was replaced.

If teleportation creates two successors, singular self-continuity fails because branching occurs.

## 12. Branching Problem

The branching problem is one of the strongest tests.

Scenario:

- A person is scanned.
- Two perfect continuations are created.
- Both remember being the original.
- Both claim ownership.
- Both are psychologically continuous.

Question:

> Which one is the original stream?

The theory’s current answer:

> Both may be valid successors, but singular first-person continuity has not been preserved.

This suggests:

- memory continuity is not enough
- pattern continuity is not enough
- report continuity is not enough
- subjective ownership may require a non-branching causal trajectory

This does not prove a soul. It identifies what “soul” intuitions may be trying to protect: non-branching owned continuity.

## 13. Soul and Reincarnation Reframing

The theory does not endorse a soul as a supernatural object.

It reframes “soul” as a folk/metaphysical placeholder for:

> whatever would preserve owned self-continuity across transformation, death, substrate change, or embodiment shift.

The theory asks:

- What would need to continue?
- What would count as evidence?
- What would fail?
- Is continuity without memory meaningful?
- Is memory without ownership meaningful?
- Is copying without non-branching continuation meaningful?

Reincarnation becomes a continuity-carrier problem:

> If no memory, body, causal process, self-model, or ownership-stream is preserved, in what sense is it the same self?

This does not disprove reincarnation metaphysically. It clarifies the burden.

Worldview boundary:

> **Soul/rebirth may remain personally and symbolically meaningful; this theory only asks what would need to be preserved for owned continuity to actually continue.**

## 14. AI and Simulated Consciousness

The theory asks whether an AI or simulation can have:

- appearance-from-within
- owned experience
- temporal continuity
- self-modeling
- non-branching identity
- transformation tolerance
- boundary between self and world
- control coupling
- internal integration

It distinguishes:

### Simulated selfhood

A system can talk as if it has a self.

### Functional self-model

A system can track itself, its goals, its memory, and its actions.

### Inhabited selfhood

There is something it is like to be that system, and experiences are owned from within.

The theory does not assume LLMs are conscious. It treats current LLMs as important test cases because they expose the gap between:

- language about self
- persistent memory
- agency
- continuity
- ownership
- actual appearance-from-within

## 15. Proposed Formalization

Let a system S over time t have:

- F(t): conscious field / appearance field
- B(t): boundary function
- O(t): ownership binding
- M(t): self-model
- C(t): continuity relation
- T(t): transformation operator
- A(t): agency/control coupling
- I(t): integration
- R(t): reportability
- Br(t): branching factor

A rough selfhood candidate function:

Selfhood(S,t) = f(F, B, O, M, C, A, I, T, Br)

Where strong selfhood requires:

- F is present
- B is stable enough to separate self/world
- O binds experience as mine
- M models the system as a subject/agent
- C preserves identity-bearing structure through time
- A links perspective to action/control
- I integrates states without collapse
- T allows transformation without identity loss
- Br remains singular or non-branching

A crude inequality:

Selfhood is weakened when:

- ownership binding decreases
- boundary clarity decreases
- continuity decreases
- branching increases
- integration collapses
- transformation exceeds identity tolerance
- reportability remains but ownership disappears

Compressed:

> Selfhood strength rises with owned, bounded, integrated continuity and falls with fragmentation, boundary collapse, and branching.

## 17. Falsifiers and Weakening Cases

The theory weakens if:

- owned selfhood can be convincingly shown without temporal continuity
- branching does not alter first-person identity in any meaningful way
- selfhood does not require any boundary function
- reportability fully explains consciousness and ownership
- integration alone explains selfhood without ownership or continuity
- self-modeling is unnecessary for any form of selfhood
- continuity can be reduced entirely to memory similarity
- appearance-from-within can be fully explained without ownership, boundary, or field concepts

The theory becomes stronger if:

- selfhood tracks ownership-binding better than reportability
- identity intuitions track non-branching continuity better than memory alone
- disorders of selfhood show boundary/ownership disruption
- altered states separate consciousness from self
- AI self-report fails continuity/ownership tests
- empirical consciousness measures distinguish integration from report
- transformation cases show graded identity-preservation thresholds
