# Thought Experiments

Canonical identity cases run against the Inhabited Continuity Theory. Each entry includes setup, prediction, what would weaken the theory, and unresolved issues.

The theory's central prediction in all cases: **owned continuity requires an unbroken, non-branching causal thread preserving boundary, integration, and self-model. Surface continuity (name, substrate, memory pattern) is neither necessary nor sufficient.**

---

## Teleportation

**Setup:** A teleporter disintegrates the original body and brain at location A, transmits a complete information pattern, and reconstructs a perfect physical duplicate at location B. The duplicate wakes with all memories and personality intact. The original is destroyed.

**Theory prediction:** The duplicate is a new entity. Owned continuity was severed at the moment of disintegration. The duplicate has accurate memories of being the original, but those memories belong to another's history — the causal thread was broken. This is not death-and-rebirth; it is death-and-replacement.

**What would weaken the theory:** If the operator's own considered intuition, after careful reflection, is that the teleported person is the same self, the philosophical primitive fails its primary test. Or if a compelling account of "pattern continuity = owned continuity" is produced that handles all other cases without branching problems.

**Unresolved:** Whether the "slowness" of disintegration matters. If atoms are replaced one at a time rather than all-at-once, does the threshold shift? The theory currently has no specification for this.

## Duplication / Branching

**Setup:** A scan is taken without destroying the original. Two perfect copies are created simultaneously. Both awaken with identical memories and personalities. Both claim to be the original.

**Theory prediction:** Neither is the original. The original owned-continuity stream does not survive into two bodies. Both are valid new entities with inherited histories, but singular first-person continuation has not been preserved in either. This is not "one of them is the real one" — it is "the original is gone, two new ones exist."

**What would weaken the theory:** If one copy demonstrably inherits more causal structure (e.g., the original body is kept and one copy is created), and the theory has no principled way to say the original-body occupant retains continuity while the copy does not. Or if asymmetric branching is the normal case and the theory has no account of it.

**Unresolved:** What about non-simultaneous duplication? If the original continues for five years and then a copy is created, does the original now have continuity and the new copy is clearly new? The theory would predict yes — but this asymmetry needs to be made explicit.

## Ship of Theseus

**Setup:** Every plank of a ship is replaced one at a time over years. No gap in the ship's existence occurs. The question is whether the resulting ship is the same ship.

**Theory prediction:** Continuity is preserved. The ship case maps to gradual neuron replacement — continuous replacement without gap, non-branching, preserving structural invariants. The resulting ship is the same ship if its structural invariants survive. If the replacement used different wood with different structural properties and the ship's function changed, it becomes a boundary case.

**What would weaken the theory:** If intuitions strongly prefer "different ship" even with continuous non-branching gradual replacement, the continuity requirement is not tracking intuitions correctly.

**Unresolved:** What counts as an invariant for the ship? The theory specifies invariants for persons (boundary, interior signal, owned continuity) but not for artifacts. The analogy requires a separate account of artifact identity.

## Brain Upload

**Setup:** A brain is scanned in full detail and the pattern is instantiated in a computational substrate. The biological brain is then destroyed. The upload reports continuous experience.

**Theory prediction:** Depends on whether the process is continuous and non-branching. If the brain is destroyed first and then the upload is activated, it is a teleportation case — the causal thread was broken, and the upload is a new entity. If the upload runs in parallel with the biological brain (gradual replacement), it approaches the Ship of Theseus case — but the parallel-running period constitutes branching, which the theory treats as continuity-breaking.

**What would weaken the theory:** A convincing account of how the upload process could be genuinely continuous (no causal gap) without any branching phase. If this is technically achievable, it would require the theory to accept substrate independence for the thread.

**Unresolved:** Can a computational process have genuine owned continuity? The theory does not rule this out — it only requires that the continuity conditions be met. Whether computation can satisfy them is an open question, not a foregone conclusion.

## Backup Restore

**Setup:** A person's brain state is backed up at time T. The person continues living. At time T+10 years, the person dies. The backup is restored, creating a copy with memories up to T.

**Theory prediction:** The restored copy is a new entity. The original's owned-continuity stream ended at death. The restored copy begins a new stream with an inherited history up to T. The copy is missing 10 years of the original's history — it cannot be the same self because it has no continuity with the original's post-T experiences.

**What would weaken the theory:** If the 10-year gap is considered acceptable (similar to how we accept continuity through sleep) — but the theory would distinguish: sleep involves no causal gap; backup-restore involves a genuine causal severance. If this distinction can be collapsed, the theory is in trouble.

**Unresolved:** What about rolling backups with very short intervals (e.g., continuous backup and restore every millisecond)? At the limit, this approaches continuous substrate replacement. The theory needs a threshold specification.

## Simulation Fork

**Setup:** A simulated mind is running. The simulation is forked: one copy continues on the original hardware, one copy runs on a different server. Both develop independently.

**Theory prediction:** Branching occurs at the fork point. Neither subsequent simulation has full ownership continuity with the pre-fork simulation. This is the clearest branching case — explicit, simultaneous, parallel streams.

**What would weaken the theory:** If simulations are not considered relevant subjects for the theory (they might not have owned experience in the first place), the thought experiment becomes moot rather than falsifying. The theory needs to specify whether it applies to simulated minds.

**Unresolved:** Whether the theory can even address simulated minds without first answering whether they have owned experience at all.

## Amnesia

**Setup:** Severe anterograde amnesia — the person cannot form new long-term memories. Each day begins fresh. Severe retrograde amnesia — prior memories are lost. The causal thread (biological continuity) is intact.

**Theory prediction:** Self-continuity is preserved at the causal level. The person is still the same self in the owned-continuity sense — the thread was not severed. Memory access is impaired, but the thread itself continues. Others correctly identify the person as the same individual. Current-moment ownership and perspective remain.

**What would weaken the theory:** Evidence that severe amnesia functionally constitutes a new entity — the person behaves in every way as a new self with no connection to the prior individual, and cannot maintain any form of ownership binding even in the present moment.

**Unresolved:** Whether a severely amnesiac person who can no longer maintain any self-model has lost "selfhood" in some degraded sense even if causal continuity is preserved. The theory may need to distinguish gradations of selfhood rather than treating it as binary.

## Sleep and Waking

**Setup:** A person undergoes dreamless sleep — hours of no reportable experience. They wake with memories intact and a sense of continuous self. Was the self continuous during sleep?

**Theory prediction:** The causal thread was maintained through sleep (biological continuity unbroken, no branching). Self-continuity is preserved. Whether experience was present during dreamless sleep is unknown — but even if it wasn't, the thread survived. The waking sense of continuity is not merely constructed; it tracks a real causal continuity.

**What would weaken the theory:** Evidence that the waking sense of continuity after sleep is entirely constructed — a confabulation with no correspondence to any real thread. If continuity is purely a narrative built after the fact, and dreamless sleep involves genuine causal discontinuity at some level, the theory's predictions for sleep become fragile.

**Unresolved:** Whether "causal continuity" through unconsciousness is functionally equivalent to a very slow backup-restore with no gap, or whether the biological process is genuinely unbroken in the relevant sense.

## Reincarnation

**Setup:** After death, a self-continuity stream is somehow preserved and re-instantiated in a new body, with no biological continuity, no memory access (typically), no causal chain (typically). The new person is claimed to be the "same soul."

**Theory prediction:** By the theory's requirements, reincarnation as typically conceived fails to preserve owned continuity: no causal thread, no memory, no biological continuity, and in most accounts, no self-model preservation. The reincarnated entity would be a new entity, not the continuation of the prior one. The "soul" concept, reframed as a portable continuity carrier, cannot currently specify what it would carry or how.

**What would weaken the theory:** A coherent account of a non-physical, non-causal continuity carrier that satisfies the theory's other requirements without a physical causal thread. The theory does not rule this out in principle — it simply cannot specify what it would be.

**Unresolved:** Whether causal continuity requires physical causal continuity, or whether some other form of causal connection could do the work. This is a deep metaphysical open question.

## Split-Brain

**Setup:** The corpus callosum is severed. The two hemispheres develop independent behavioral streams. The left hemisphere (typically speech-capable) produces coherent narrative self-reports. The right hemisphere responds with different choices and preferences.

**Theory prediction:** Non-branching continuity is disrupted. The pre-surgery unified self does not survive in either hemisphere in full. Both hemispheres are valid continuants — they have real experience and real behavioral agency — but neither is "the original unified self" in the theory's full sense. The left hemisphere's narrative interpreter produces a confabulated sense of unity.

**What would weaken the theory:** If one hemisphere clearly inherits full, unambiguous, non-confabulated continuity of the pre-surgery unified self, while the other clearly does not — and this asymmetry is stable. The theory would then need an account of how continuity can be asymmetrically preserved under branching.

**Unresolved:** Gazzaniga's research suggests the left hemisphere confabulates unity. But whether it is fully confabulating or partially tracking a real (reduced) continuity is disputed.

## Dissociation

**Setup:** Severe dissociative identity disorder — multiple identity states alternate, each with distinct personality, memories (sometimes), and preferences. A single biological body and brain hosts apparently multiple "selves."

**Theory prediction:** The owned-continuity structure is disrupted or fragmented. Whether there are multiple distinct owned-continuity streams or one disrupted stream with multiple modes is an open question for the theory. The boundary and integration conditions are clearly altered.

**What would weaken the theory:** If all dissociative identity cases can be fully explained as one damaged self with no genuine fragmentation of owned continuity — just alternating access to different behavioral patterns. Or conversely, if each identity state is clearly a full self, which would require the theory to account for multiple selves in one body.

**Unresolved:** Whether "alters" have their own owned continuity streams or share a degraded common one. The theory does not currently have a clean answer.

## Gradual Neuron Replacement

**Setup:** Every neuron is replaced over 20 years by functionally identical synthetic equivalents. The process is continuous — one neuron at a time, no gaps, no branching.

**Theory prediction:** Self-continuity is preserved. The replacement is continuous (no causal gap) and non-branching (no simultaneous original and copy). If the structural invariants of the system survive, the self survives. Substrate independence is affirmed: what the neurons are made of does not matter; what the continuity-preserving process did matters.

**What would weaken the theory:** If intuition strongly resists identity preservation even under continuous non-branching gradual replacement — if "real neurons" feel like a necessary condition regardless of process continuity. This would suggest substrate dependence overriding structural invariants.

**Unresolved:** At what granularity does "gradual" become "not-gradual"? If the replacement happens neuron-by-neuron but each replacement involves a brief functional gap, does that break the thread? The theory needs a threshold for gap-tolerance.
