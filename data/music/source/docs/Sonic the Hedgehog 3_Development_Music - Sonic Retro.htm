    History

    Development

    Michael Jackson and Hisashi Suzuki during one of Jackson's trips to Sega of Japan.
    “ ... With Sonic 1 and 2, we were working with Nakamura-san, so we couldn't get any extra music, but since with 3 we had the music done in-house, we were able to have a whole lot of music made up for us. We picked out from that selection the music that fit each zone best. „

    — Takashi Iizuka, Sonic Jam Official Guide[1]

    Work on the Sonic the Hedgehog 3 soundtrack initially began in-house, with Sega veteran Tokuhiko "Bo" Uwabo responsible for managing the game's sound development, assigning Sega staff to production work, and contracting outside composers as needed.[2] A number of composers working for the "Sega Sound Team" began contributing tracks for the game, including Tatsuyuki Maeda and Tomonori Sawada, the latter providing the original theme song and its derivative jingles.[3][4] Yoshiaki "MILPO" Kashima and Masaru Setsumaru were tasked with refining the game's sound engine,[5] Setsumaru also focusing on sound programming and arrangement.[2] While not directly composing for Sonic 3, one of Kashima's songs previously created for the unreleased arcade title SegaSonic Bros. was repurposed into Sonic 3's Special Stage theme.[6] Jun Senoue, who was busy with Formula One World Championship: Beyond the Limit, made small contributions by writing the game's bonus stage themes.[7] Masayuki Nagao, still a Sega employee but in the middle of moving over to sound company Opus, was brought over for arrangement work and creating remixes for every second act of a zone.[2] At some point, the company Cube was commissioned to compose eight additional songs for the game.[8]

    Sometime in 1993, pop artist Michael Jackson became involved in the music production of Sonic the Hedgehog 3.[9][10] Jackson was no stranger to Sega, having previously worked with the company on Michael Jackson's Moonwalker and Michael Jackson in Scramble Training. There are conflicting reports as to how he became part of the project. According to Roger Hector, then-head of Sega Technical Institute and Executive Coordinator of Sonic 3,[11] Jackson was a passionate fan of Sonic the Hedgehog - especially the games - and had reached out to Sega in early 1993 with a desire to meet the developers. Upon being introduced by Hector to Sonic Team at STI, one of the staff assigned to Sonic the Hedgehog 3[9] suggested to the artist that he compose the game's soundtrack; Jackson agreed instantly.[12]

    “ "I get a phone call from someone who is telling me to expect a visit from Michael Jackson at STI. I said, "Really? And what's that all about?" I got no more information. It turns out Michael Jackson was a real heavy-duty Sonic fan. He bought all the games. He had them all. He played them all. He loved Sonic more than anything else, and he wanted to come to meet the team that did that because he respected that. He wanted to expose himself to the team. That's what he wanted.


    Once I had that explained that way, I said, "Well, okay, come on down." He and his bodyguards and such arrived in the afternoon after work. We didn't want to attract any media into that. This was just a private intro thing and showing- giving Michael Jackson, as a fan, the opportunity to meet the team. So he came, I showed him around. He was very soft spoken, very nice guy. No fancy celebrity characterization at all. We wound up in our conference room with the Sonic Team and I introduced them. I said, "This is Michael Jackson. You've probably heard of him and this is the Sonic Team," and they all joined into their own conversation. Ultimately one of the guys in the Sonic Team said, "Hey, Michael, why don't you do the music for the next Sonic game?" And he thought about it for about two seconds and said, "Okay, I'll do it all." Normally that kind of negotiation would take place between attorneys across a span of weeks, right? Now, this is just done."

    		„

    — Roger Hector[12]

    However, according to Mayumi Nina Sakazaki, Jackson's longtime Japanese coordinator, the idea of Jackson working on Sonic 3 was posed by project manager Hisashi Suzuki.[10] Suzuki, who had become friends with Michael Jackson after the singer first visited Sega of Japan back in the late 1980s,[13] told Jackson about Sonic 3 and asked if he would be interested in writing the soundtrack. Immediately, Jackson accepted. Though Suzuki warned it would not pay as well as his other endeavors, he promised Jackson that if the game was successful, he'd find some way to reward his efforts.[10] Surprisingly, Jackson refused his offer of reimbursement, instead requesting that Sega donate a percentage of the game's profits to charity.[14]

    As recorded in STI artist Craig Stitt's daily work journal, Jackson visited Sega Technical Institute on March 10, 1993 to discuss the details of working on the project.[15] The artist was walking on crutches during his visit,[16] having sprained his ankle two days prior during rehearsals for the 1993 Soul Train Music Awards. During this trip,[16] he flew a group of Sega representatives, which included Yuji Naka and Hirokazu Yasuhara,[16] to Neverland Ranch in his personal helicopter[17] and treated them to lunch. Jackson was gifted a Sega letterman jacket during this visit, with Sega of America later installing commemorative footprints in the spot Jackson had walked.[16] Sega audio head David Javelosa also recalls Jackson making a personal visit to STI to drop off assets related to the project. Still on crutches from his March 8th injury,[18] the artist took the opportunity to tour Sega Multimedia Studio (where he was reportedly unimpressed with the in-development Jurassic Park) and Spencer Nilsen's audio facilities,[19] "We took him down to the studio, introduced him to Spencer, and for about the next hour watched as this whole other person emerged."[19]

    Initially, Hisashi Suzuki described the game's various Zones verbally, which gave Jackson a difficult time imagining what sounds could accompany them. However, once Suzuki switched to picture-based examples, Jackson sprung to life and began envisioning the game's music. According to Suzuki, Jackson was the type of artist whose creativity was inspired by "visuals, not words."[14] Eventually, Jackson was given an early demo of the game.[9] To help with the project, Jackson contacted musician Brad Buxer. First working with Jackson on the 1991 Dangerous album, he later became Jackson's musical director. According to Buxer, Jackson "wasn't up for doing a whole lot,"[20] placing Buxer in charge of the project.[9][21] The first two artists to join Brad Buxer and Michael Jackson were Bobby Brooks and Doug Grigsby III, two musicians who not only worked with Jackson in the past but were also sharing a house with Buxer at the time. Soon enough, Darryl Ross, Geoff Grace and Cirocco Jones were hired on to be a part of the Sonic 3 team.[9]

    Michael Jackson (right) assigned the project's management duties to long-time friend and collaborator Brad Buxer.

    For roughly four weeks, Jackson and his team worked out of Record One, a recording studio in Los Angeles that Jackson had recently used for Dangerous.[22] Sound engineer duties at the studio fell on Matt Forger,[9] a role he had played on previous Jackson projects starting with 1982's Thriller.[23] While Brad Buxer claims that Michael Jackson only worked on one cue with him,[24] Cirocco Jones claims that Jackson would call at all hours with ideas and melodies for the game. "I actually have "ALL" of the tracks, from the original humming of Michael calling in the middle of the night leaving messages, to his ideas at Record One with Matt and Bruce."[25] When recording, Jackson would use his "beatbox style," and during down time would sit in a small room playing video games, including Sonic the Hedgehog.[9]

    A demo tape of Jackson's beatboxing cues was sent to Sega of Japan, where Naoto Ohshima was able to listen to them early in the development process. The tape, described by Ohshima as consisting entirely of Jackson's mouth-made sounds - including drums, horns, melodies, and his trademark vocalizations - was reportedly a complete multi-layered vocal demo. As of 2018, Ohshima believes Sega still retains the tape, though it has never been released or digitized.[26]

    “ [The process] wasn't as we would normally construct songs for an album or another project of Michael's ... We were recording lots of beatboxing. Lots of Michael's mouth percussion. ... He'd be laughing, joking, and that kind of infectious attitude would ... make the work not seem like work. Michael understood that this was for a game, he was in a really up mood whenever we'd be working. „

    — Matt Forger, Michael Jackson's sound engineer[9]

    Together, the group recorded 41 cues,[20] some of them being completely original compositions, others based on previous work. The most famous of the latter was what became the IceCap Zone theme, originally an unreleased song called "Hard Times" by Buxer's 1980's New Wave band The Jetzons.[27][28] Some other recycling of past material included "Blood on the Dance Floor," a single recorded in 1991 co-produced by Buxer and Jackson which features a rolling drum beat later included in "Knuckles’ Theme" and the "Act 1 Mini-Boss Theme," as well as the direct sampling of at least three previous Jackson songs ("Why You Wanna Trip On Me," "Jam," and "In the Closet")[29] - all tracks produced for the album Dangerous. All the songs were recorded "high-profile," treating them as any other Michael Jackson song.[9] At one point, Jackson also wanted the song "Black or White" to be used in Sonic 3, but "due to opposition from his lawyers" the song was not included.[30]

    Eventually, Sega's in-house Sega Sound Team, along with Jackson's assistant Mayumi Nina Sakazaki and Sega's Hisaki Nimiya, were flown to a Los Angeles hotel with their computers and equipment to produce the soundtrack with Jackson.[31] This process consisted of working with the artist to create demo tracks with professional instruments during the day and then translating that music to FM synthesis for playback on the Mega Drive during the night. Jackson and his team would then listen to the results and provide feedback.[21] The Sega Sound Team assumed they could complete the soundtrack in about a week, but problems acquiring a satisfactory sound from the new compositions, as well as ideological differences between the two parties and issues with sample memory size, resulted in the team remaining in Los Angeles two weeks longer than expected.[32][33]

    One of Buxer's biggest obstacles was transcribing Jackson's style to digital music. In particular, Buxer ran into conflicts with the Sega Sound Team over the shifting of rhythms, as he was told the limitations of video game music at the time resulted in more rigid "beats". Worse, Jackson was reportedly disappointed in the sound quality of the translated compositions, telling Buxer his work could never be released in this state. In response, Nimiya countered that millions of Jackson's fans listened to his work on low-quality speakers such as radios, and the group endeavored to produce the best quality they could from such a unique collaboration.[33] According to several people involved with the project, Jackson always remained professional and polite and never spoke down to other musicians.[34]

    “ "I guess he was there signing or doing some contracts or talking about doing the music for Sonic 3 and was a huge Sonic fan and wanted to meet the team. And we knew about it. We knew he was coming. So we were all in the conference room and it's kinda surreal because I'm siting there, and at that point, that's like the high point of Michael Jackson. He's like the most famous person on the planet I remember sitting there and thinking, "There are people who would literally kill me to be sitting here."


    I think I asked him one question, mostly it was just him asking questions and talking to people. Most of the time was focused on Naka and Yasuhara and maybe Yamaguchi."

    		„

    — Craig Stitt[16]

    The Sega Sound Team continued to work on Jackson's tracks after flying back to Japan. In September of 1993, while in Japan during his Dangerous tour, Jackson met back up with the Sega Sound Team regarding final approval of his team's contributions.[33] Still unhappy with the sound, he asked why earlier Sonic games had better music, Nimiya telling him it was a memory allocation issue. Rejecting one track during this meeting, he told Nimiya he would write another song, then inviting the Sega staff to his September 11, 1993 concert at the Fukuoka Dome.[35]

    STI's Howard Drossin, from the 1994 MTV television special Rock the Rock.

    On September 14, 1993, while in Moscow, Michael Jackson was accused of sexual misconduct involving a minor, with a lawsuit filed by the child's father. Distraught, Jackson asked for Buxer to come to his hotel room. Thinking he wanted to talk about the Sonic 3 soundtrack, he brought a cassette with the cues, but Michael refused to listen. Instead, he asked Buxer to play on a piano in the room. Performing one of the cues in "a much different way," the musical exchange between the two resulted in the writing of "Stranger in Moscow," which would end up on Jackson's next album.[20]

    Even with the legal drama, Jackson's team was never told to stop working on the tracks for the game. Their work for the game soon concluded, with the final tracks being sent to Sega.[9] Shortly before the allegations, one final musician was brought in to work on the Sonic 3 soundtrack from within Sega, Howard Drossin. Getting the assignment, Drossin was initially told that he would work with Jackson, but not long after was told Jackson was off the project.[9] Howard's work would end up in Sonic & Knuckles, only getting a "Special Thanks" credit in Sonic 3.[36][37] Additionally, Sega of America management held a meeting in the STI offices with some of the company's audio staff, where David Javelosa was told "We're burying Michael Jackson's tracks. So we need new tracks to go up at the front."[18]

    Just like his joining the project, there are conflicting reports as to the true reason for Michael Jackson's departure, and subsequent lack of credit, on Sonic the Hedgehog 3. According to Roger Hector, "his work was dropped after his scandals became public."[38] Brad Buxer had a different explanation as to why Jackson forwent credit, stating that the artist "was not happy with the [resulting] sound coming out of the console. [The Genesis] did not allow an optimal sound reproduction, and Michael found it frustrating. He did not want to be associated with a product that devalued his music."[39] Tom Kalinske, CEO of Sega of America at the time, stated in a later interview that the decision for Michael Jackson to step away from the project was mutual, and not a push from Sega to remove him.[40] Though Jackson's name is nowhere to be found, the rest of Jackson's team was credited for their work.[41][42][43]

    “ "So Michael agreed to do the music for Sonic 3 and that resulted in him doing an amazing job. The actual soundtrack for Sonic 3 had the Michael Jackson sound to it, but it also sounded appropriate for a Sonic game. It was about as perfect as I could imagine it to be. Unfortunately, very shortly before the Christmas-season launch of the game, there was some bad PR about Michael Jackson that caused the Sega company to go, "Oh, no, no. We can't be a part of that." So that fell on me to get my own in-house music composer, who was really good but not well known, to actually remove all of the existing music in the Sonic game and replace it. We had to keep him there day and night [sarcastically] passing food under the door, y'know? He had this impossible task to accomplish and he took it on and he did it and it was great.


    The first six months were invested in making it all wonderful through Michael Jackson, but it didn't work out."

    		„

    — Roger Hector[12]

    Legacy

    Title screen for the Sonic 3 prototype, containing music first heard by the public in Sonic & Knuckles Collection.

    In 1997, Sonic & Knuckles Collection was released for the PC. In it, a handful of tracks from Sonic the Hedgehog 3 were replaced with alternate compositions. Early fan speculation thought it had something to do with the difficulty of replicating the samples involved in the original release for a MIDI format, having different songs be an easy workaround. The final Mega Drive songs were present in Sonic Jam released later that year. The unaltered Sonic 3 was subsequently included in 2002's Sonic Mega Collection and would continue to be made available in its unaltered state in various collections and standalone re-releases through 2011.[44]

    Mention of Michael Jackson being involved in Sonic the Hedgehog 3 first came up in an interview Roger Hector had in August 2005. Unprompted, he mentioned the pop singer, saying he had been brought on to do all the music, but his contributions were removed from the game due to his sexual abuse allegations.[38] Hector reaffirmed this statement in a second interview two years later, claiming the general public had never heard the Jackson soundtrack.Media:Makingofs3kpg4.jpg[45]

    With this revelation, speculation began as to whether or not the Jackson-led tracks had actually been removed from the final game, most famously explored by Qjimbo's "Michael Jackson's Sonic 3" YouTube video.[46] In the video, connections are made between the "Carnival Night Zone" theme and the Jackson song "Jam," as well as the Sonic 3 credits music and "Stranger in Moscow." The discovery of Cirocco Jones' biography stating he worked with Jackson on the game's soundtrack was also uncovered at roughly the same time.[47]

    The connection between Jackson and Sonic 3 was further solidified in December 2009 by Jackson fan magazine Black & White, which featured an interview with Brad Buxer. In it, he confirmed Jackson's involvement, while also contradicting Roger Hector's previous recounting of events. According to Buxer, Jackson asked to have his name left off the game due to being unsatisfied with how the Mega Drive replicated the songs. He also confirmed that at least one composition from those sessions was in the game - the credits sequence of Sonic 3 - and that it eventually evolved into the Jackson track "Stranger in Moscow."[39]

    Roger Hector, who first confirmed Jackson's involvement in Sonic the Hedgehog 3.

    In 2011, Dissident93 reached out to Cirocco Jones, asking about his work on Sonic 3. Confirming his involvement, he later said "I believe that there is a lawsuit going on or about to go on. Sega owes a lot of people money from the Sonic stuff. Speak with Bobby Brooks... He may tell you a LOT more about everything....."[48] Contacting Bobby Brooks for further clarification on this possible lawsuit, he was rebuffed and told to speak to his lawyer.[2]

    The subject of Sonic 3's music history was brought into focus once again with an episode of GameTrailers' Pop Fiction. Talking about the history of Jackson's involvement, as well as research and interviews that had been previously done, it featured a new video interview with Roger Hector, once again claiming that Jackson's work was not in the final product.[30]

    “ Once the decision had been made to pull Michael Jackson's music from the game, we had to kind of scramble to try and replace the whole musical soundtrack. We had Howard Drossin at STI composing new music. We also had a coupleof other composers from Sega of Japan that were contributing. ... I would never say unequivocally that there was no similarity between any of the music that Michael Jackson created and what ultimately wound up in the game. But I can say that if that occurred it certainly wasn't by Design or by any intent on the part of Sega. „

    — Roger Hector, Head of STI and Executive Coordinator of Sonic the Hedgehog 3[30]

    The video also quotes an unnamed source who worked with Jackson at the time, echoing statements made by Brad Buxer in previous interviews.[30] The story of Jackson's involvement with Sonic 3 was also the focus of a 2016 article from the Huffington Post, where members of Jackson's team including Brad Buxer, Doug Grigsby III and Cirocco Jones spoke on the record about the project.[9]

    Sega's ability to use the original tracks suspected to be from Jackson's sound team was called into question with the release of the Lego Dimensions Sonic the Hedgehog level pack. While plenty of music was taken from early games, certain tracks were replaced with soundalikes, including Carnival Night and IceCap. More recent Mega Drive collections, such as Sega Mega Drive Classics refrained from including Sonic 3 & Knuckles while re-releasing other Sonic titles from the Mega Drive. It wouldn't be until 2022's Sonic Origins that Sonic 3 would see another release. A remake of the game using the Hedgehog Engine, a handful of tracks including Carnival Night, IceCap, and Launch Base Zone were replaced, instead using the songs first heard in the Sonic & Knuckles Collection. These tracks had been discovered to predate the original PC port, appearing in the 1993-11-03 prototype of Sonic the Hedgehog 3.

    Sonic Studio Social Team manager Katie Chrzanowski[49] spoke of the music replacement on Sonic Official season 6, episode 3: "While unfortunately we can’t use all the original sounds from the Sega Genesis version of the game, Jun Senoue has been working really hard to adapt the original music that was composed in 1993 for Sonic Origins, [...] going so far as reproducing it, with the same sound chip from the Sega Genesis, and using his own digital audio tape collection to make this as faithful to the originals as possible."[50]
