## 0. Scope and Safety Boundary

This domain describes the operator’s embodied and sensory profile.

It covers:

- DOMS/muscle soreness enjoyment
- massage gun use
- stretching/pandiculation
- localizable body awareness
- body as a sensor map
- sensory gating
- heat/light/noise sensitivity
- darkness preference
- controlled loud audio
- random noise threat
- heights / spatial exposure
- fast unpredictable movement threat
- fidgeting, pacing, rocking, leg bouncing
- standing/walking work effects
- body size/build context
- sensory overlap with food, aesthetics, music, and software

This domain is not medical advice.

Safety boundary:

- sharp pain
- sudden swelling
- bruising without cause
- numbness/tingling
- weakness
- pain that worsens instead of fades
- chest pain
- shortness of breath
- calf swelling/redness/warmth
- injury after trauma
- symptoms that disrupt normal function

should be treated as medical flags, not profile material. The domain tracks the operator’s normal preference/sensation profile, not a permission slip to ignore injury. The nervous system is not a vibes-based diagnostic tool, tragically.

Best rule:

> **Pleasurable soreness belongs here; injury does not.**

## 3. Core Reward Mechanics

### 3.1 Localizable Body Map

The strongest body reward comes when sensation becomes spatially specific.

Soreness is interesting because it says:

```text
this exact region participated
this tissue is recovering
this movement had a body consequence
this part of the map is lit up
```

The operator often describes soreness as a heat map.

This is crucial. The pleasure is not just the sensation. It is the interpretability.

Best compression:

> **A sore muscle is a highlighted map region.**

### 3.2 DOMS As Positive Signal

DOMS is liked when it is:

- dull
- muscular
- delayed
- localizable
- non-sharp
- non-injury-like
- tied to known activity
- improved by light massage/stretch
- felt as evidence of use/recovery

Examples:

- hamstring soreness
- calf/soleus soreness
- serratus anterior soreness after box-stacking
- shoulder soreness after heavy work
- plantar/bottom-foot soreness after standing/walking
- localized soreness when scratching/massaging the area

Why it feels good:

- confirms the body was used
- makes muscle anatomy perceptible
- gives a recoverable signal
- allows relief through massage
- turns abstract physical labor into mapped feedback
- feels like healing/strengthening rather than damage

Best compression:

> **DOMS is pleasurable because it turns work into a readable body map.**

### 3.3 Massage / Pressure / Vibration

Massage is rewarding when it modulates a local signal.

The massage gun is appealing because it provides:

- controlled intensity
- local pressure
- vibration
- mechanical relief
- agency over sensation
- repeatable targeting
- clear before/after contrast
- body-map exploration

Light massage is preferred over aggressive force. The goal is not to destroy the tissue like an idiot with a power tool. The goal is to make the local signal move.

Best compression:

> **Massage is field editing for muscle sensation.**

### 3.4 Stretch / Pandiculation

Stretching is rewarding because it:

- lengthens a local line
- reveals muscle/tendon continuity
- creates controlled tension
- makes body geometry perceptible
- releases built-up pressure
- can create whole-body reset
- feels animal-like in a good way

Pandiculation-like stretching — the full-body, involuntary-ish stretch/yawn-style extension seen in cats/dogs and human morning stretches — is especially compatible because it blends:

```text
stretch
+ nervous-system reset
+ muscle contraction
+ tremor/vibration
+ release
```

Best compression:

> **Stretching turns body geometry into sensation.**

### 3.5 Controlled Intensity

The body/sensory system can like intense input when it is chosen. The newer resolution is that intensity itself is not the primary problem; unmodeled, unchosen, or socially imposed input is.

Chosen intensity:

- loud music
- bass pressure
- massage gun
- stretch tension
- soreness relief
- dark room
- cold air
- challenging games
- authored danger
- physically demanding work when meaningful
- high-volume audio replacing ambient noise
- bounded text or chat around a shared object

Unchosen intensity:

- random banging
- surprise calls
- rapid-fire messages that turn text into live obligation
- fluorescent glare
- heat
- traffic volatility
- sudden insects/bats
- unpredictable human motion
- forced public attention
- bright exposed spaces
- social performance pressure

Best compression:

> **Intensity is not the problem. Unowned signal is.**

Second compression:

> **Chosen signal can be intense; unchosen signal feels invasive because it removes ownership and prediction.**

## 4. Sensory Gating

### 4.1 Sound

Sound is high-weight.

Controlled loud sound can be regulating.

Examples:

- loud music
- headphones
- foobar setup
- bass-heavy playback
- controlled immersive audio
- spatial DSP when tuned
- car music/podcasts

Uncontrolled sound is intrusive.

Examples:

- random banging
- sudden thumps
- other people’s conversations
- traffic noise
- fluorescent hum
- chaotic public noise
- ambiguous source sounds
- repeated unexplained impact

The issue is not decibels.

It is:

```text
chosen signal vs invading noise
```

Best compression:

> **Controlled loud sound is signal; random banging is trespass.**

### 4.2 Light

Light is high-weight.

Preferred:

- darkness
- screen-only light
- warm low light
- controlled contrast
- overcast diffusion
- night conditions
- low glare

Rejected:

- fluorescent lighting
- bright sterile spaces
- harsh sun
- glare
- post-snow reflection
- warehouse/office institutional light
- bright hot open spaces

Darkness works as sensory compression.

Best compression:

> **Darkness reduces the world to chosen signal.**

### 4.3 Heat / Temperature

Heat is hostile.

Known pattern:

- sweats easily
- warm environments cause irritation
- itching/agitation in heat
- prefers cool air
- roughly 55°F outside feels good
- below 70°F indoors is preferred
- slightly chilly = alert/comfortable
- warm = physically irritating

Heat fails because it is full-body unchosen signal. It cannot be localized or ignored.

Best compression:

> **Heat is ambient hostile input.**

### 4.4 Smell

Smell appears lower-weight unless extreme.

This matters because sensory profile is channel-specific. The operator can be highly sensitive to sound/light/heat without being equally smell-sensitive.

Best compression:

> **Sensory sensitivity is not evenly distributed across channels.**

### 4.5 Motion / Trajectory

Fast unpredictable movement is threatening.

Examples:

- jumping bugs
- bats
- sudden flying things
- erratic small motion near the body
- traffic agents
- chaotic humans
- untrusted PvP intrusion

Controlled speed is attractive.

Examples:

- football breakaways
- Rocket League
- Jayden Daniels extending plays
- fast music with readable rhythm
- Souls movement when authored
- night driving when controlled

Best compression:

> **Controlled speed is exhilarating; unmodeled speed is threat.**

### 4.6 Spatial Exposure

Heights are aversive because they create non-inhabitable depth.

Features:

- exposed vertical void
- catastrophic consequence from small movement
- loss of enclosure
- gravity as active threat
- body near irreversible transformation

Best compression:

> **Heights are non-inhabitable depth.**

## 5. Body Awareness Anchors

### 5.1 Hamstrings

Hamstring awareness is high.

Known details:

- strong awareness of semitendinosus region
- soreness near back of knee / tendon region
- visible/felt hamstring mass
- interest in hamstring tendon split near popliteal fossa
- awareness that muscle transitions into tendon gradually, not as two totally separate objects
- gait-related soreness can feel pleasurable when non-injury-like

Best compression:

> **Hamstrings are one of the clearest body-map regions.**

### 5.2 Calves / Soleus

Calf soreness is strongly liked.

Soleus interest makes sense because:

- it is deep
- endurance-related
- heavily involved in standing/walking
- less obvious than gastrocnemius
- often felt through sustained work rather than showy contraction

Best compression:

> **The soleus is attractive as hidden support infrastructure in the body.**

### 5.3 Serratus Anterior

Serratus soreness after stacking heavy boxes overhead was highly legible.

This region is interesting because it is:

- not a glamour muscle
- involved in scapular movement
- felt around ribs/side body
- activated by reaching/stacking/pushing overhead
- easy to become aware of once sore

Best compression:

> **Serratus soreness reveals the shoulder blade’s hidden machinery.**

### 5.4 Plantar / Foot Muscles

Bottom-of-foot soreness is interesting when it is not plantar fascia pain but local muscle soreness near toes.

The operator distinguishes:

- arch/fascia
- toe-side small muscles
- sole tension
- cramp risk vs pleasurable contraction
- standing/walking work effects

Best compression:

> **Foot soreness is interesting when it maps small hidden muscles, not vague foot pain.**

### 5.5 Tendon/Muscle Transitions

The operator is interested in how muscle becomes tendon.

This fits the global pattern:

```text
soft tissue
→ transition zone
→ tendon
→ attachment
→ force transfer
```

The attraction is to anatomical continuity and phase change.

Best compression:

> **Tendons are structural continuity made visible at the body’s edges.**

## 8. Sensory Ownership Model
The model now explicitly includes social and communication input. Phone calls, surprise questions, rapid reciprocal texting, and unpredictable human volatility can function like sensory intrusions because they seize timing, attention, and future obligation before the operator has consented to the loop.


The central body/sensory distinction:

```text
chosen input = regulatable
unchosen input = invasive
```

Chosen input can be intense:

- loud bass
- massage gun
- cold air
- spicy/texture food events
- hard game pressure
- deep work
- dark room
- controlled movement

Unchosen input can be mild but hostile:

- faint random banging
- unexpected phone call
- fluorescent light
- warm room
- social gaze
- unpredictable motion
- public exposure

Best compression:

> **The nervous system accepts intensity when it owns the threshold.**

## 10. False Positives / Negative Controls

### 10.1 “Likes pain”

False.

The operator likes controlled, meaningful, localizable soreness/pressure. Injury pain, sharp pain, random pain, and threatening pain do not belong.

### 10.1a “Text preference means all texting is easy”

False. Text passes because it can preserve pacing and control. It fails when the other person treats a reply as permission for immediate back-and-forth, unknown questions, or a new obligation chain.

### 10.2 “Dislikes loudness”

False.

Controlled loudness can regulate. Uncontrolled noise violates.

### 10.3 “Is fragile”

False.

The profile is ownership-sensitive, not intensity-avoidant.

### 10.4 “Gym person”

False.

Body awareness is high, but gym culture, exposure, performance, and weightlifting identity have low pull.

### 10.5 “Heat preference because comfort”

No.

Warm colors pass; warm ambient temperature often fails.

### 10.6 “Dark room means sadness”

False.

Darkness is sensory compression and focus architecture.

### 10.7 “Massage gun should be stronger”

No.

Light, controlled pressure is better than aggressive overuse.

### 10.8 “All soreness is good”

False.

Only known, muscular, dull, localizable, non-injury-like soreness passes.

Best compression:

> **Predict ownership and localization, not sensation category.**
