## 0. Scope and Ground Rules

This domain describes food and drink as a first-class taste / experience domain.

Food is not the operator's most intellectually important interest. It is important because it exposes a simpler, more embodied version of the same architecture visible in music, games, aesthetics, attraction, body sensation, and workspace design.

Food does **not** need hidden depth to matter. Sometimes the whole structure is:

```text
fast
+ familiar
+ satisfying
+ low mess
+ right texture
+ no unwanted extras
```

This domain should preserve:

- favorite foods and drinks
- plain/simple meal preferences
- convenience and low-prep priorities
- texture preferences
- temperature preferences
- dessert anchors
- fast-food / drive-thru anchors
- microwave / air fryer / easy-cooking anchors
- coffee and cold drink patterns
- seasonal, holiday, and return patterns
- exact topping/sauce boundaries
- vegetable preparation boundaries
- dessert-form boundaries
- personal food taxonomy rules
- food-specific false positives
- autistic-common food selectivity / sameness / sensory-gating context
- what is comfort, what is boredom, and what is too much
- how food feeds the global cognitive map

This domain should not:

- become a moral diet plan
- pretend food preference is only nutrition
- reduce everything to fast food
- reduce everything to comfort food
- reduce everything to novelty
- erase plain/simple meal preference
- erase Thanksgiving / autumn / holiday/seasonal mechanics
- ignore texture and temperature
- collapse the operator's taste into generic “likes junk food”
- assume vegetables fail entirely
- assume sauces are broadly welcome
- medicalize eating behavior without reason
- turn every food into a metaphor when the sensory surface is already enough
- pretend every pattern here is uniquely personal when many are autistic-common
- call every bread-enclosed object a sandwich like a taxonomic barbarian

Core rule:

> **Describe the taste mechanics before explaining them.**

Food is one of the best places to keep the map honest. Not every preference here requires mystery, reconstruction, hidden topology, or intellectual depth. Sometimes the attraction is simply:

```text
meat + cheese
tenders + fries
eggs + toast
grilled cheese
pizza bagels
cold creamy coffee
thick shake by spoon
```

Tiny miracle: the grand ontology occasionally has to admit that a bacon cheeseburger with ketchup and mustard is not asking to be deconstructed like a lost civilization. Rude, but fair.

## 1. What This Domain Is to the Operator

Food is a first-class experience domain because taste, texture, comfort, holiday/seasonal, convenience, and bounded abundance expose reward mechanics that are not reducible to simple favorite-food labels.

The central food pattern is:

```text
simple stable base
+ satisfying density
+ minimal unwanted extras
+ controlled texture event
+ low preparation friction
+ returnability
```

Food becomes strongest when it is:

- familiar enough to trust
- simple enough not to become noisy
- fast enough to be worth the effort
- rich enough to feel rewarding
- bounded enough not to become mess
- textured enough to create events
- seasonal, holiday-linked, or returnable enough to carry memory
- cold/creamy enough to regulate
- modular only when the base remains intact

Best compression:

> **Food works when comfort has structure and effort stays low.**

The food domain is less about “depth” and more about **grammar**.

A plain sandwich can work because it does not make the bite negotiate with lettuce, tomato, onion, sauce, and humanity's eternal need to make things wetter than they need to be.

A bacon cheeseburger can work because it is a stable field:

```text
bun + meat + cheese + bacon + ketchup/mustard
```

A tenders-and-fries meal can work because it is simple, hot, salty, crisp/soft, and requires no fake sophistication.

Thanksgiving can work because it is abundant but coherent.

The disliked form is not simplicity.

The disliked form is:

> **extra stuff that makes the food less itself.**

## 2. Autism / Food Selectivity Context

A major clarification:

> **A lot of this maps cleanly onto autistic food taste / sensory food selectivity. The operator is not unique for having the pattern; the domain value is in recording the exact local parameters.**

Autistic-common food mechanisms relevant here:

- preference for predictable foods
- restricted or selective food range
- sensitivity to texture, taste, smell, temperature, and appearance
- preference for sameness / repeatable meals
- rejection of mixed or messy foods
- strong aversion to particular textures
- reliance on safe foods
- difficulty with prep friction and food planning
- comfort in familiar food routines
- stronger reactions to small ingredient or texture deviations than outsiders may expect

This does not mean every food preference is caused by autism or that the operator's taste is clinically unusual. It means the food map should treat predictability, texture, sameness, and preparation friction as load-bearing rather than as quirks.

Important boundary:

```text
autistic-common mechanism
    !=
generic autistic food profile
```

The shared mechanism is broad. The operator's actual profile is local:

```text
plain meat/cheese sandwiches
tenders + fries
bacon cheeseburgers with ketchup/mustard
hot dogs with ketchup/mustard or chili
fried/stir-fried/cooked vegetables > raw vegetables
broccoli and green beans as positive vegetable anchors
spinach on pizza as a compatible embedded vegetable
pepperoni pizza
chicken + pineapple pizza
Thanksgiving foods broadly
brownies/cookies > cake
cold creamy coffee
thick spoonable shakes
easy home foods only
```

Best compression:

> **The pattern is common; the parameter settings are personal.**

Second compression:

> **Autism explains why predictability and texture matter; the domain explains which predictions and textures pass.**

## 3. What This Domain Is Not

Food preference is not:

- only fast food
- only comfort food
- only junk food
- only sugar
- only convenience
- only peak taste
- only dopamine slop
- only calories
- only childhood nostalgia
- only restaurant availability
- a sign that the operator lacks taste
- a contradiction of the larger cognitive map

This domain should reject several lazy reductions.

### 3.1 Not “just fast food”

Fast food appears often because it is:

- available
- predictable
- drive-thru compatible
- late-night or post-work compatible
- low social friction
- reliable enough to repeat
- structured around familiar grammars
- low prep
- low cleanup
- fast conversion from hunger/craving to result

But the pattern also includes Thanksgiving foods, pumpkin pie, brownies, cookies, cheesecake, mac and cheese, cold brew, thick milkshakes, pizza variants, eggs, toast, grilled cheese, broccoli, green beans, spinach-on-pizza, and simple sandwiches.

Fast food is one access layer. It is not the whole preference architecture.

### 3.2 Not “peak taste above all”

A major correction:

> **Convenience often matters more than peak taste.**

The operator tends to eat fast food, microwavable food, and air-fryer food because they are fast and satisfying enough. Cooking can fail before taste because it requires:

- prep work
- planning
- cleanup
- timing
- ingredient management
- counter-space / dish mess
- executive-function overhead
- guessing what future self will want

The issue is not that cooked food cannot taste better. It often can. The issue is that the full cost of cooking includes planning and cleanup, and the payoff often does not beat the friction.

Compression:

> **A meal is better when it exists quickly and does not leave a mess ecosystem behind.**

### 3.3 Not “random modularity”

Earlier food modeling overemphasized modular variation. The correction:

> **The operator likes stable food grammars, but often prefers them plain.**

Variation works in desserts, pizza forms, milkshake add-ins, Thanksgiving plates, and some fast-food formats. But for sandwiches, burgers, and hot dogs, the preferred pattern is usually narrow and simple.

Good:

- bacon cheeseburger with ketchup/mustard
- cheeseburger if bacon is unavailable
- hot dog with ketchup/mustard
- chili dog
- sandwich with only meat and cheese
- tenders + fries
- plain breakfast sandwich grammar

Bad:

- onions
- raw vegetables on burgers/sandwiches
- salad-like freshness inserted into handheld comfort food
- too much sauce
- too many toppings
- “loaded” versions that turn the food into a wet committee meeting

Compression:

> **Plain is not empty; plain protects the main signal.**

### 3.4 Not “health vs unhealthy”

This document is not making nutrition claims. It is mapping subjective reward.

A food can be nutritionally boring and structurally rewarding.  
A food can be nutritionally “better” and taste-mechanically dead.  
A “healthy substitution” can fail if it breaks the texture, density, ease, or expected payoff.

This does not mean nutrition is irrelevant to life. It means nutrition is not the organizing variable of this domain.

### 3.5 Not “vegetables are rejected”

Vegetables are not globally rejected.

Preferred vegetable mode:

```text
fried / stir-fried / cooked / embedded in warm savory food
```

Weak vegetable mode:

```text
raw / fresh / salad / burger topping / sandwich topping
```

Positive vegetable anchors:

- broccoli
- green beans
- spinach on pizza
- fried vegetables
- stir-fried vegetables

The issue is not “vegetable” as a category. It is preparation, texture, temperature, and context.

Broccoli works as a positive vegetable anchor. Green beans pass strongly in Thanksgiving context. Spinach on pizza works because it is embedded into a warm cheese/sauce/crust grammar instead of acting like a raw wet interruption. Stir-fried or fried vegetables can pass because heat, oil, salt, browning, and texture integration make the vegetable feel like part of the meal grammar.

Compression:

> **Vegetables pass better when they are cooked into the field, not dropped onto it raw.**

## 4. Core Food Model

The strongest model is:

```text
plain/simple meal grammar
+ convenience gate
+ bounded abundance
+ soft/dense base
+ sharp/texture event
+ holiday/seasonal or return
+ temperature fit
```

### 4.1 Plain/simple meal grammar

The operator tends to prefer plain or simple meals.

Strong forms:

- chicken tenders + fries
- sandwich with only meat and cheese
- bacon cheeseburger with ketchup/mustard
- cheeseburger if bacon is unavailable
- hot dog with ketchup/mustard
- chili dog
- eggs and toast
- grilled cheese
- pizza bagels
- microwavable / air-fryer foods that preserve expected texture
- Thanksgiving plate items that are familiar and compatible

The “plain” preference is not a lack of taste. It is a gating preference:

```text
remove noise
preserve main payoff
avoid incompatible textures
avoid surprise ingredients
keep the bite legible
```

Best compression:

> **Plain meals preserve signal integrity.**

### 4.2 Convenience gate

Convenience is not secondary. It is one of the strongest gates.

A food is more likely to be chosen if it is:

- fast
- predictable
- easy
- low prep
- low cleanup
- microwavable
- air-fryer compatible
- drive-thru compatible
- satisfying without planning
- repeatable without ingredient management

Cooking often fails because the preparation field is too expensive:

```text
plan
shop
prep
cook
monitor
clean
store leftovers
deal with dishes
```

The operator will cook or prepare things when the process is simple enough:

- pizza bagels
- toast
- eggs
- grilled cheese

These pass because they are fast, bounded, familiar, and satisfying.

Compression:

> **Food that requires a project plan has already lost.**

### 4.3 Bounded abundance

The operator likes abundance only when it has a boundary.

Good abundance:

- Thanksgiving spread
- pizza variants
- milkshake add-ins
- dessert families with compatible variants
- familiar fast-food choices
- breakfast sandwich forms
- burger/hot dog grammar when toppings stay narrow

Bad abundance:

- too many incompatible flavors
- too much mess
- too many options without hierarchy
- novelty buffet with no identity
- overloaded toppings
- chaotic menu sprawl
- “more” that makes the base less legible

Compression:

> **Abundance is attractive only when the field has a stable grammar.**

### 4.4 Soft/dense base

Many preferred foods have a soft, dense, comforting base:

- pumpkin pie filling
- cheesecake
- brownies
- cookies
- mac and cheese
- mashed potatoes
- rolls
- pizza cheese / crust / sauce system
- burger bun + meat + cheese
- breakfast sandwich egg/cheese/bread structure
- banana pudding shake
- thick milkshake body
- cold creamy coffee drinks
- fries when the interior is soft
- hot dog bun + meat + sauce grammar
- grilled cheese

The soft base is the continuity layer. It carries the food and makes it safe to re-enter.

Compression:

> **Softness is the edible floor.**

### 4.5 Sharp / controlled event

Preferred foods often add a sharp or controlled texture/flavor event inside the soft base:

- graham crust against pumpkin pie
- brownie edges / chew
- cookie edge / chew / chips
- wafers in banana pudding shake
- M&Ms in thick shake
- Snickers pieces in thick shake
- crisp fry exterior
- bacon in burger / breakfast sandwich
- pizza crust contrast
- pepperoni as salty/spicy event
- pineapple with chicken as sweet/tangy event
- spinach on pizza as cooked embedded vegetable event
- ketchup/mustard tang
- chili on hot dog
- cheese pull / sauce / crust relation
- crunch add-ins against cream
- cold coffee sweetness/cream balance

The event should not destroy the base. It should punctuate it.

Compression:

> **Texture events work when they interrupt without breaking continuity.**

### 4.6 Holiday field and return

Food becomes stronger when it returns through holiday/seasonal:

- Thanksgiving
- autumn foods
- pumpkin pie
- repeated cold coffee cravings
- night coffee runs
- post-work fast food
- drive-thru routines
- favorite milkshake orders
- holiday food repetition
- seasonal dessert return

The holiday setting makes food more than a single sensory event. It gives it a field.

Compression:

> **Holiday return turns food into a re-enterable room.**

## 5. Master Anchor: Thanksgiving

Thanksgiving is the cleanest food-domain anchor, but it should **not** be described primarily as a ritual. The pull is more concrete and less mystical than that. It should also not be overread as strong family-contact motivation: the holiday passes through autumn, food, football, familiar plate grammar, and seasonal return more than kinship obligation.

Thanksgiving works because it combines:

- many favorite foods at once
- autumn timing
- cool-season atmosphere
- football as a built-in holiday backdrop
- familiar warm foods
- compatible plate grammar
- desserts that already fit the operator's taste
- abundance that is bounded by a specific seasonal food field
- low novelty pressure: the foods are familiar, expected, and repeatable

Thanksgiving is not just “lots of food.”

It is:

> **favorite-food abundance in the middle of autumn with football attached.**

This distinction matters. Thanksgiving would lose much of its pull if it were moved to March, disconnected from football, and rebuilt around seafood. The attraction is not the abstract holiday category. It is the exact convergence:

```text
favorite foods
+ autumn
+ football
+ warm sides
+ pies
+ familiar table grammar
```

Protected Thanksgiving anchors:

- turkey
- rolls
- mac and cheese
- pies
- pumpkin pie
- green beans
- mashed potatoes
- Thanksgiving dessert table broadly
- football atmosphere / Thanksgiving football as part of the field

Rejected or weak holiday-adjacent foods:

- macaroni salad
- potato salad
- seafood-centered holiday food
- cold mayo-salad formats
- raw/fresh salad logic as default

The macaroni/potato salad rejection matters because it sharpens the boundary: pasta/potato/starch are not automatically liked. The cold mayo-salad format likely breaks the warm/soft/familiar comfort grammar and introduces a texture/sauce profile that does not fit.

Critical correction:

> **The preference is not abundance of possibility-space in the abstract. It is favorite-food abundance inside the right season, sport atmosphere, and familiar warm-food field.**

Best compression:

> **Thanksgiving works because it is autumn + football + nearly all favorite foods.**

Second compression:

> **Thanksgiving passes because almost everything on the plate belongs to the same warm autumn field.**

Third compression:

> **If Thanksgiving were seafood in March with no football, the core attraction would collapse.**

## 6. Food Taxonomy Rules

The operator has explicit category boundaries. These matter because food categories are not just labels; they predict texture, construction, and expectation.

### 6.1 Sandwich

A sandwich requires:

```text
two slices of bread
+ filling in the middle
```

High-confidence sandwiches:

- meat-and-cheese sandwich on two slices of bread
- grilled cheese
- standard two-slice deli sandwich

Preferred sandwich grammar:

```text
bread
+ meat
+ cheese
```

Usually not wanted:

- sauce
- vegetables
- onions
- lettuce/tomato
- wet freshness
- excess topping noise

Compression:

> **A sandwich is two bread planes with filling between them.**

### 6.2 Not sandwiches

The following are **not** sandwiches in this taxonomy:

- hot dog
- hoagie / sub if built from one split roll
- Pop-Tart
- pupusa
- steamed bun
- other filled/wrapped objects

Reason:

```text
one bread object split or wrapped
    !=
two separate slices with filling in the middle
```

A hot dog bun is one bread object sliced partly open. A Pop-Tart is enclosed pastry. A pupusa is filled dough. A steamed bun is filled/wrapped dough. These are related food architectures, but not sandwiches.

Compression:

> **Filled or folded bread is not automatically a sandwich.**

Second compression:

> **A hot dog is a filled bun object, not a sandwich.**

This taxonomy is not just internet argument bait, although obviously it can be used for that, because society deserves consequences. It also preserves expectation boundaries. A hot dog is eaten and topped differently from a sandwich. A Pop-Tart is a filled pastry object. A pupusa or steamed bun is filled dough. Calling all of them sandwiches destroys useful category resolution.

### 6.3 Why this matters

Food taxonomy predicts:

- texture
- eating method
- topping logic
- mess expectation
- temperature expectation
- filling distribution
- structural failure mode
- whether sauce/toppings belong
- what kind of “plain” is expected

Good food categories preserve operation.

Bad food categories flatten everything into clever technicalities.

Compression:

> **A food category should predict how the object behaves.**

## 7. Plain Meals and Handheld Grammar

The operator tends to prefer simple, legible meals with narrow topping boundaries.

### 7.1 Tenders + fries

Tenders + fries are a strong plain-meal form.

Why they work:

- simple protein + side
- hot
- salty
- crispy exterior
- soft interior
- air-fryer / fast-food compatible
- no complex toppings
- easy to eat
- predictable
- low cognitive load

Compression:

> **Tenders + fries are plain payoff with crisp/soft structure.**

### 7.2 Meat-and-cheese sandwiches

Preferred sandwich grammar:

```text
two slices of bread
+ meat
+ cheese
```

Usually not wanted:

- sauce
- vegetables
- onions
- lettuce/tomato
- wet freshness
- excess topping noise

Why it works:

- legible bite
- stable texture
- low mess
- no surprise wetness
- no raw vegetable interruption
- no flavor clutter

Compression:

> **A sandwich works when meat and cheese stay in charge.**

### 7.3 Bacon cheeseburgers

Preferred burger grammar:

```text
bun
+ meat
+ cheese
+ bacon when available
+ ketchup
+ mustard
```

If bacon is unavailable, a plain cheeseburger still passes.

Usually rejected:

- onions
- raw vegetable toppings
- excessive sauce
- loaded burger logic
- toppings that make the bite wetter or less legible

Bacon matters because it adds:

- salt
- crispness
- smoke
- sharp event
- savory density

Ketchup/mustard matter because they provide a bounded sauce/tang layer without turning the burger into sauce soup.

Compression:

> **Bacon cheeseburger = simple savory base plus crisp salt and controlled tang.**

### 7.4 Hot dogs

Hot dogs are not sandwiches in the operator's taxonomy.

Preferred hot dog forms:

- ketchup + mustard
- chili dog

Rejected:

- onions
- loaded toppings
- raw crunch that interrupts
- too much topping sprawl

Hot dogs work because they are compact, simple, and sauce-bounded.

Compression:

> **A hot dog works when the topping event stays inside the bun grammar.**

Second compression:

> **Not a sandwich. Still good. Civilization survives.**

## 8. Pizza Grammar

Pizza is one of the strongest modular food grammars, but the topping map is still selective.

The base grammar:

```text
crust
+ sauce
+ cheese
+ heat
+ topping event
```

Preferred pizza forms:

- pizza bagels
- delivery pizza
- frozen pizza
- stuffed crust
- thin crust
- Bagel Bites
- Hot Pockets
- pizza rolls

Go-to toppings:

- pepperoni
- chicken + pineapple
- spinach on pizza

### 8.1 Pepperoni

Pepperoni is the default topping event:

- salty
- fatty
- slightly spicy
- crisp edges possible
- integrates cleanly with cheese/sauce
- does not make the pizza wet in a raw-vegetable way
- keeps the pizza recognizably pizza

Compression:

> **Pepperoni is the cleanest pizza event.**

### 8.2 Chicken + pineapple

Chicken + pineapple works as a controlled sweet/savory contrast:

- chicken provides familiar protein
- pineapple provides sweet/tangy event
- heat integrates pineapple into the pizza field
- contrast is strong but still inside stable crust/sauce/cheese grammar

This is important because it shows that the operator is not anti-sweet/savory or anti-fruit in food. The issue is whether the addition integrates into the field.

Compression:

> **Chicken + pineapple works because the sweet event is bounded by pizza grammar.**

### 8.3 Spinach on pizza

Spinach on pizza often works because the vegetable is:

- cooked
- softened
- embedded in cheese/sauce
- warm
- integrated into the pizza field
- not acting like raw salad dumped onto a hot meal

This sharpens the vegetable rule.

Compression:

> **Spinach passes when pizza turns it from raw leaf into warm embedded texture.**

### 8.4 Pizza variation boundary

Pizza can tolerate many forms because its identity kernel is strong.

But pizza can still fail when:

- toppings become too numerous
- onions dominate
- raw vegetables create wet/fresh interruption
- sauce overload breaks structure
- crust becomes soggy
- novelty replaces pizza identity

Compression:

> **Pizza variation works when crust/sauce/cheese remain the center of gravity.**

- spinach on pizza
- fried vegetables

These pass because they can be:

Weak or rejected:

- can be simple without being bland

Compression:

> **Broccoli is a vegetable that becomes a stable cooked side instead of raw noise.**

### 9.4 Green beans

Green beans pass especially in Thanksgiving / cooked meal context.

Likely why they work:

- warm
- familiar
- seasonal/holiday compatible
- simple side grammar
- compatible with mashed potatoes, turkey, rolls, mac and cheese

Compression:

> **Green beans work when they belong to the warm plate field.**

Best vegetable compression:

> **Cook the vegetable into the meal; do not use it as cold decorative interference.**

## 10. Seafood Boundary

Seafood is a major negative-control category.

The operator strongly dislikes most seafood, especially forms that preserve too much visible animal body structure, require cracking/opening, smell strongly, look wet/slimy, or require messy extraction work.

Strong seafood rejections:

- shrimp
- crawfish
- oysters
- octopus
- squid
- most shellfish
- seafood boils
- fish heads / intact fish-body presentation
- opening crab bodies
- cracking crab legs for small amounts of meat
- messy shell extraction
- wet, mixed seafood piles

The seafood-boil image is especially low-pull because it combines multiple rejection mechanics at once:

```text
wetness
+ shells
+ animal-body visibility
+ mess
+ strong smell implication
+ extraction labor
+ low yield per effort
+ chaotic pile presentation
```

Best colorful compression, because accuracy sometimes requires violence:

> **A seafood boil looks like somebody boiled the contents of a wet dumpster.**

### 10.1 Fish exception

The operator does not reject all fish categorically.

Fish can pass when it is processed into a simple meal grammar:

- fish and chips
- fried fish + fries
- grilled fish + fries
- grilled/fried fish + rice
- mild processed fish without visible head/body confrontation

The fish exception works because the fish is converted into a legible cooked-food object instead of presented as an intact animal-body puzzle.

Compression:

> **Fish passes when it becomes a simple cooked meal, not when it stares back as biology.**

### 10.2 Crab boundary

Crabmeat can be acceptable when already processed.

Rejected:

- cracking crab legs
- opening crab bodies
- digging for tiny pieces of meat
- shell mess
- body-interior visibility
- work-to-meat ratio

Possible pass:

- already-processed crabmeat
- crab incorporated into a non-messy prepared food, if the seafood flavor/smell does not dominate

The issue is not only taste. It is:

```text
mess
+ labor
+ low yield
+ body disgust
+ visual/tactile boundary violation
```

Compression:

> **Crabmeat is more acceptable when the animal-body work has already been removed.**

### 10.3 Why seafood fails

Seafood often fails before flavor because it violates several high-level food gates:

- visual disgust
- body-interior exposure
- wetness/slime implication
- smell implication
- messy hands
- extraction labor
- low payoff per effort
- unpredictable texture
- visible animal structure
- social/table mess
- too much work during eating

This connects directly to the convenience gate. Eating should not become a task where the reward is hidden inside a corpse puzzle. Yes, evolution invented shells. No, that does not mean dinner has to be an archaeological dig through ocean armor.

Best compression:

> **Seafood fails when it is wet, visibly anatomical, messy, and labor-intensive.**

Second compression:

> **Processed fish can pass; creature-disassembly seafood fails.**

## 11. Dessert Architecture

Desserts are major anchors.

Protected dessert anchors:

- pumpkin pie
- brownies
- cookies
- cheesecake
- thick milkshakes
- banana pudding shake
- M&M McFlurry
- mini McFlurry
- banana split in a cup
- Cook Out cheesecake / strawberry / blueberry / banana pudding variants

Core correction:

> **Brownies and cookies are preferred far more than cake.**

Dessert preference is not simply “sweet.”

The preferred dessert structure usually includes:

```text
dense base
+ sweetness
+ texture contrast
+ creaminess or chew
+ optional crunch
+ strong identity
```

### 10.1 Brownies

Brownies are a major dessert anchor.

Why they work:

- dense
- chewy
- chocolatey
- structurally compact
- edge/interior contrast
- strong payoff without needing icing
- easy to understand as a dessert object

Compression:

> **Brownies are dense chocolate compression.**

### 10.2 Cookies

Cookies belong near brownies as a preferred dessert form.

Why they work:

- handheld
- bounded
- chewy/crispy variation
- chips or mix-ins as controlled events
- no need for icing
- stable snack grammar
- easy repeat
- texture contrast built into the object

Compression:

> **Cookies are bounded sweetness with built-in texture events.**

### 10.3 Cake / icing boundary

Cake is weaker than brownies and cookies.

Why cake often fails or is only okay:

- cake often needs icing to be good
- icing is overused
- icing can become too sweet, too thick, or too dominant
- cake crumb alone can feel less dense/payoff-rich than brownies/cookies
- frosting can turn the dessert into sugar layer rather than structure

The operator is not broadly anti-cake, but cake is not a protected anchor the way brownies/cookies/pumpkin pie/cheesecake are.

Compression:

> **Cake often needs icing to matter, and icing often overplays its hand.**

### 10.4 Pumpkin pie

## 26. Research Anchors

These are background anchors only. They should not override direct preference evidence.

Useful concepts:

- **autistic food selectivity** — supports treating predictability, restricted range, texture, taste/smell sensitivity, and food refusal as common autistic food mechanisms.
- **oral sensory sensitivity** — supports texture/smell/taste/temperature as load-bearing variables.
- **sensory-specific satiety / variety effect** — helps explain why variety can change food reward, but this domain sharpens that into bounded variety, not unlimited variety.
- **texture-specific sensory reward** — supports treating crunch, creaminess, thickness, and mouthfeel as core rather than secondary.
- **palatability / hedonic food reward** — supports separating nutritional need from pleasure, craving, holiday/seasonal, and reward.
- **comfort food / nostalgia** — supports holiday/seasonal and memory effects, but should not reduce the whole domain to childhood comfort.
- **sensory contrast** — supports the soft base / sharp event model.
- **context and cue effects** — supports drive-thru/night/cold-coffee routine as part of food reward, not mere logistics.
- **executive-function cost** — supports preparation/planning/cleanup as part of food access, not laziness or mere preference.

Important boundary:

> **Research can explain why the mechanics are plausible; it does not decide which foods the operator likes.**

Best compression:

> **Science can name the machinery; the archive tells us what the machinery selects.**
