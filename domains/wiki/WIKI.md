## 9. Core Article Architecture

A strong article is organized as a layered object.

Generic preferred anatomy:

```text

infobox / image / skimmable identity surface

subsections with chronological or conceptual order

notes

navboxes / categories / authority control / structured data support
```

But the operator does not apply this mechanically. The anatomy shifts by article type.

The deeper rule:

> **Article structure should match the subject's causal anatomy.**

A sports biography is built around life/career chronology and statistical residue.  
A game article is built around gameplay, development, release, reception, legacy.  
A stadium article is built around history, financing, design, site, future use.  
A business/sports-owner biography is built around early life, career, ownership, initiatives, personal/philanthropy.  
A template page is not a narrative article at all; it is interface infrastructure.

Best compression:

> **The page should behave like the subject.**

## 10. Lead-Body Architecture

The lead is the article's entry chamber.

It should:

- identify the subject
- state what kind of subject it is
- give enough context for a general reader
- establish why the subject matters
- summarize the body with proper weight
- avoid trivia, hype, and teaser-writing
- avoid facts that the body cannot reopen
- be shorter than the body but not vaguer than the body
- compress without deleting the subject's identity

The operator's best compression:

> **A lead is only good if the body can still reopen it.**

This means:

```text
lead claim
→ body section that supports/reopens it
→ citations that verify it
→ article architecture that lets the reader inspect it
```

The lead fails when:

- it contains orphan facts
- it overweights a minor detail
- it has no clear body support
- it reads like a news lede
- it uses inflated diction
- it summarizes without orientation
- it is technically accurate but gives no handle
- it hides the subject's real structure behind smooth prose

Best compression:

> **The lead is not decoration; it is a compressed index of the article's body.**

---

## 11. Body Structure: Chronology, Causality, and Reader Route

The body should provide the route that the lead promises.

Preferred body-section logic:

- chronological when the subject develops through time
- conceptual when the subject has stable components
- causal when a development requires explanatory sequence
- nested when subsections clarify rather than fragment
- compressed when details are redundant or low-yield
- expanded when the detail is load-bearing
- sourced at the level where the claim lives

The operator tends to preserve:

- chronological order
- career phase boundaries
- season-level boundaries when they change state
- source-backed developments
- statistics and awards as structured residue
- tables only when they reveal state better than prose
- notes when they prevent overloading the main text
- external links only when they function as public access points

The operator tends to reject:

- paragraph splits that fragment one idea
- sections that exist only because a detail exists
- bloated prose that repeats what the table already gives
- detail piles that do not change the reader's model
- excessive season/game/event minutiae without article-level weight
- source claims that technically exist but do not support the exact text
- current-language claims that go stale quickly
- standalone trivia energy

Best compression:

> **The body should increase resolution, not scatter the map.**

## 19. Source / Citation Model

Sources are not decorative.

A source should:

- directly support the claim
- be reliable enough for the claim type
- match the level of specificity
- support dates and chronology
- avoid laundering speculation into fact
- be placed near the claim it supports
- allow future readers/editors to inspect the evidence route
- distinguish official sources, news reports, databases, interviews, and primary sources
- avoid citation clutter when the body can carry the support cleanly
- avoid “source exists somewhere” logic

The operator's instinct:

> **A citation is load-bearing only if it opens the exact door the claim uses.**

This is why wrong citation dates, unsupported birthplace statements, age-overreliance, and current-language drift are irritating. They are not cosmetic failures. They make the public object less trustworthy.

Best compression:

> **Citations are door hinges, not wallpaper.**

### 19.1 Source Lineage

The lineage session sharpened a wiki-specific pattern: sources are not merely detachable proof tokens. In a good article, sources preserve the route by which a claim can be reopened.

The article's public reader should be able to move from:

```text
surface claim
→ article section
→ citation
→ source context
→ date / author / institution / evidence type
→ reason the claim is justified
```

This is especially important for historical, biographical, technical, sports, and language-related claims, where the same surface sentence can hide very different evidentiary histories.

Examples of source-lineage differences:

- official roster page vs news report
- primary interview vs secondary profile
- box score/stat database vs narrative article
- historical source vs modern summary
- policy/MOS rule vs local consensus
- official game page vs fan wiki
- Japanese source vs English localization
- live report vs later retrospective

A citation fails when it technically points somewhere but does not preserve the reader's ability to reconstruct the claim.

Best compression:

> **A citation should reopen the claim’s lineage.**

Second compression:

> **Verification is stronger when the reader can see what kind of source-world the claim came from.**

### 20.1 Revision History, Oral Memory, and Temporal Lineage

Wiki work exposes a public version of the time/continuity problem.

A page is not only its current prose. It is a current state with a recoverable path behind it: revisions, sources, talk pages, templates, images, Wikidata, page moves, merges, trims, reverts, and citation lineage.

This matters because public knowledge can persist through:

- written documentation
- source archives
- revision history
- shared standards
- human memory
- oral transmission
- AI summaries and downstream reuse

Oral stories and memes are adjacent continuity cases: information can persist without one stable written record, but the cost is mutation. Wikipedia reduces mutation by making lineage inspectable.

Best compression:

> **A wiki page is public memory with a revision spine.**

Second compression:

> **Oral memory preserves through minds; wiki memory preserves through lineage.**

False positive:

> Treating current article text as the whole object. The living public object includes the path that made the current text trustworthy.

## 22. Compression vs Flattening

This is a critical boundary.

Good wiki compression:

- removes redundancy
- removes unsupported detail
- trims over-specificity
- clarifies chronology
- consolidates scattered source material
- tightens sentence function
- keeps lead/body alignment
- preserves the subject's identity
- makes future reader orientation easier
- makes future editor maintenance safer

Bad wiki compression:

- deletes load-bearing examples
- removes context needed for reader understanding
- smooths prose until the subject loses texture
- hides causality
- removes necessary chronology
- makes a lead impossible to reopen
- overcompresses for the average reader
- turns an article into a sterile summary

The operator's known weakness:

> **Overcompression pressure: carrying slightly too much structure per sentence because the internal model tolerates more density than the average reader.**

This weakness is domain-relevant because the operator likes compression, but Wikipedia demands compression that survives public reader inference.

Best compression:

> **Compression is good only when the reader can reopen the subject.**

## 26. Page Organization Principles Extracted From Authored Pages

### 26.1 Start with identity, then reveal internal structure

### 26.2 Match sections to subject behavior

The page anatomy changes by subject type:

- stadium: history → financing → design → surrounding development
- business owner: early life → career domains → ownership → personal/philanthropy
- template: interface surface → documentation → TemplateData → transclusions

Best compression:

> **Sections should be subject-derived, not template-derived.**

### 26.3 Separate narrative from structured residue

Good articles separate:

```text
prose = what happened / why it matters
tables = structured residue
infobox = skimmable identity state
references = verification route
navboxes/categories = public map connections
```

### 26.4 Preserve phase boundaries

Phase boundaries are important:

- college teams
- NFL seasons
- development vs release
- financing vs design
- private equity vs sports ownership
- current vs former roles

These boundaries prevent articles from becoming fact soup.

### 26.5 Keep special analysis in its own safe section

`Player profile` is the clearest example: it gives style/skill information a proper home instead of scattering it through chronology.

Analogous possible sections:

- `Gameplay` for game mechanics
- `Design` for stadium physical/conceptual form
- `Management style and initiatives` only when not PR
- `Legacy` for long-tail residue
- `Esports` when competitive life is central

### 26.6 Keep personal/private context bounded

Personal life belongs only when it is relevant, sourced, and proportional.

Best compression:

> **Context belongs; gossip does not.**

### 26.7 Make current public state explicit

For current subjects, the page must show state:

- current team
- current role
- current plan
- current status
- current season
- planned vs built
- active vs former
- signed vs expected

Best compression:

> **Currentness is not trivia when the page is a public state object.**
