## Development History

### Project Origins

*Sonic 3* and *Sonic & Knuckles* were originally planned as a single game. Manufacturing costs of a 34-megabit cartridge with NVRAM made that prohibitive, and Sega of America's commitment to a McDonald's Happy Meal promotion in February 1994 forced an early split. The *S&K* lock-on cartridge was created specifically so the complete game could still be experienced as intended.

Development began January 1993. The team initially attempted a Sega Virtua Processor-based 3D isometric game (working title *Sonic 3D*); when the chip wouldn't be ready by 1994, the project restarted as a conventional 2D platformer. The isometric concept eventually became *Sonic 3D Blast* (1996).

### The Jackson Sessions

**Initiator:** Hisashi Suzuki (Sega of Japan), who had befriended Jackson during a late-1980s Sega collaboration. This contradicts the version where Roger Hector claims the connection came through Jackson being a *Sonic* fan (which is also true — Jackson had previously worked with Sega on the 1990 arcade *Moonwalker*).

**Confirmed date:** March 10, 1993 — Craig Stitt's journal records Jackson visiting STI.

**Team assembled by Brad Buxer** (Jackson's tour director/musical director since *Dangerous*, 1991):
- Bobby Brooks — audio engineer
- Darryl Ross — audio engineer
- Geoff Grace — arranger
- Doug Grigsby III — composer, bassist (died 2021)
- C. Cirocco "Scirocco" Jones — composer, arranger, sound programmer (died 2021)

**Studio:** Record One, Los Angeles (same facility used for *Dangerous*). Engineer: Matt Forger. Duration: ~4 weeks.

**Workflow:** Demo tracks created with professional instruments/synths during the day → translated to Genesis FM synthesis overnight → Sega team gave feedback. Jackson recorded an a cappella demo tape (confirmed by Naoto Ohshima). The Sega team was housed at a hotel nearby with their equipment. The development team were given a helicopter tour of Neverland Ranch.

**Total output: 41 cues.** Buxer confirmed this; he composed most of these cues himself. Per Buxer's 2019 podcast interview, Jackson personally worked on only one of the 41 tracks — the credits theme (which then became "Stranger in Moscow"). *Retro Gamer* notes that if accurate, it is unlikely any music composed by Jackson himself (as distinct from his team) remained in the final game.

**Cirocco Jones' role:** Jones listed himself as "Programmer, Additional Production and Arranger-Composer" for "levels 2 & 3" on a track he called "The Water" — implying Hydrocity Zone. He listed Jackson and Buxer as co-credits. The track title was removed from his website in 2008 (credits remained). However: the 1103 prototype contains Hydrocity using the Universal Voice Bank (Sega Sound Team instrumentation), inconsistent with MJ-team authorship. Sonic Mania's use of Hydrocity also contradicts MJ origin. Most likely Jones' cues went unused and he credited himself based on his submitted work, not the final game.

### IceCap Zone — The Jetzons

The IceCap Zone melody is directly based on **"Hard Times"**, an unreleased track from Brad Buxer's early-1980s New Wave band The Jetzons. Copyright documentation credits The Jetzons' vocalist/guitarist **Bruce Connole** as the song's sole writer. Buxer states he wrote the hook and that Connole's sole credit was "a mishap with the copyright." The song did not see release until 2008 (*The Complete Jetzons* compilation). Publicly discovered in 2013.

### The Credits Theme and "Stranger in Moscow"

Brad Buxer composed the S3 credits theme by himself, without Jackson's involvement. Later, at Jackson's hotel room, Buxer played a slightly rearranged version on piano. Jackson was captivated and they worked together for a couple of hours, finishing what became the 1995 Jackson single "Stranger in Moscow." The S3 credits theme and the Jackson song thus share a common origin but are separate compositions. Buxer did not receive a composition credit on "Stranger in Moscow."

The S3 credits theme uses samples of MJ beatboxing, also found in *Space Channel 5*.

### Jackson's Departure

Three conflicting accounts:

1. **Hector/Ohshima (official position):** Jackson's involvement terminated after the September 1993 child sexual abuse allegations. David Javelosa recalled being told at a Sega of America meeting: *"We're burying Michael Jackson's tracks. So we need new tracks to go up at the front."*

2. **Buxer/Grigsby/Jones:** Jackson was dissatisfied with how the Genesis reproduced his music and asked for his name removed. Buxer said they received no instruction to halt work and sent the finished soundtrack to Sega in mid-1993.

3. **Kalinske:** The decision was mutual. He also said any Jackson involvement was arranged without senior Sega's knowledge and no formal agreements were made.

The accounts are not fully reconcilable. Jackson was likely already dissatisfied with FM synthesis quality; the abuse allegations provided Sega a business reason to accelerate the departure.

**Howard Drossin** was brought in near end of Sonic 3 production, expecting to work with Jackson. He was quickly told Jackson was off the project. His compositions ended up in *Sonic & Knuckles*; he received only a "Sound Special Thanks" in *Sonic 3*. Drossin confirmed Masaru Setsumaru arranged the S&K themes (2008 SAGExpo interview).

### 1103 Prototype (1993-11-03)

Discovered in 2019. Contains NO trace of the MJ team tracks — not even the sample IDs in the sound driver. All zones replaced in the final by MJ-team versions (Carnival Night, IceCap, Launch Base) exist here using the Universal Voice Bank. This confirms the MJ tracks were wholesale replacements inserted after November 1993.

### S&K Collection PC (1997) — Why Different Music

The 1997 Windows port restored the prototype/pre-MJ tracks for CNZ, ICZ, LBZ, Knuckles Appears, Competition Menu, and 2P Select. Two compatible explanations:

1. **Access:** MIDI demos from the Sega Sound Team were internally available. The MJ tracks were never handed over as MIDI data (they were recorded like album tracks and adapted by Sega programmers). Re-requesting MIDIs from Buxer's team for a budget port wasn't practical.
2. **Technical:** The MJ tracks are heavily percussion-and-sample-driven. General MIDI couldn't reproduce the elaborate sample layers (Heavy D, Run-D.M.C., beatboxing, glass breaks) that define them. The Sega Sound Team compositions translated naturally to MIDI; the MJ material did not.

Sonic Origins (2022) used Jun Senoue's faithful re-recordings of the prototype tracks for the same zones, recorded on original Genesis hardware using Senoue's DAT tape collection.

## Analysis Layers in Detail

### Layer 1 — Causal (Chip / Driver)

**Input:** `.vgz` file (cycle-accurate YM2612 + SN76489 register write log)
**Tools:** `vgmtools`, `libvgm`, `Nuked-OPN2`
**Extracted signals:**
- FM patch parameters per channel: algorithm, feedback, operator ratios (DT/MUL), envelope (AR/DR/SR/RR/TL), SSGEG flags
- PSG channel frequency and volume envelopes
- Channel role assignment (melody / bass / percussion / pad)
- Register write density and timing (driver tick cadence, jitter)
- SMPS loop point position and return passage structure
- DAC sample usage (track 7 kicks, special effects)

**Key question:** How does each programmer implement the FM patches? Algorithm preferences, feedback levels, operator balance — measurable programmer signatures.

### Layer 2 — Symbolic (Composition / Musicology)

**Input:** VGM command stream → reconstructed MIDI-equivalent note events
**Tools:** `domains/music/model/analysis/symbolic_analyzer.py`, `codec_pipeline.py`
**Extracted signals:**
- Note pitch, duration, velocity per channel
- Interval histogram and entropy
- Scale/mode detection (Aeolian, Dorian, Mixolydian, Ionian, Lydian, etc.)
- Chord progression and harmonic vocabulary
- Rhythmic quantization and syncopation metrics
- Phrase length, regularity, and recurrence ratio
- Melodic contour and motif structure
- Loop return: harmonic/melodic state at the loop point

**Key question:** What compositional choices were made within the chip's constraints? Melody shape, harmonic language, rhythmic feel, phrase structure — independent of synthesis implementation.

### Layer 3 — Perceptual (Rendered Audio / MIR)

**Input:** Audio rendered from `.vgz` via `game-music-emu` or `libvgm`
**Tools:** MIR pipeline (`domains/music/model/analysis/track_analysis.py`)
**Extracted signals:**
- Spectral centroid and brightness
- Onset density and tempo
- Timbre cluster count (MFCC-based)
- Loudness envelope and dynamic arc
- Loop return detection; return passage segmentation
- Collapse sharpness at the loop return (`collapse_proxy`)

**Key question:** What does the listener actually hear? Timbre, energy, groove, brightness — acoustic fingerprint independent of implementation details.

## Hikichi FM Channel Layout Pattern

Sonic Retro explicitly documents the Doomsday Zone channel layout as matching Mushroom Hill and Flying Battery:

> "Doomsday Zone uses the same FM Channel layout as other Sonic & Knuckles tracks such as Mushroom Hill and Flying Battery, with the main melody being in FM 1, an echo of it in FM 5, the bassline in FM 2, and one note of a two note chord FM 3, with FM 4 playing the other note along with an arpeggiator. This track also uses 2 PSG Channels, a rarity in Hikichi's tracks for this game."

This independently documents Hikichi's channel layout as a compositional fingerprint. The "rarity of PSG usage in Hikichi tracks" aligns with the Zero-PSG Constraint in `STRONG_INVARIANTS.md`. Despite the weak Helix call (Setsumaru, margin 0.05), the channel layout evidence supersedes it — Doomsday Zone is attributed to Hikichi.

## S3K Phase 1–3 Findings: The Arranger Bias Constraint (Two Layers)

Initial Euclidean distance profiling via `s3k_fingerprint_compare.py` against uncredited `Sega Sound Team` tracks mapped heavily to **Masanori Hikichi**, **Masaru Setsumaru**, and **Yoshiaki Kashima**.

Hikichi worked for external contractor **Cube**, credited for **8 tracks** total between him and Miyoko Takaoka. Hikichi has only confirmed a subset — he does not claim all uncredited tracks.

**Critical Finding — Layer 1 (FM/Causal):** The FM patch and groove analysis shows that at the hardware layer, the programmer fingerprints of **Setsumaru and Kashima** overwhelm the original composer signal. Their patch choices and quantization habits are baked into the sequence data.

**Critical Finding — Layer 2 (Symbolic/Compositional):** A four-way compositional profile comparison (Ogawa / Sawada / Maeda / Hikichi, features: note_density, chromatic_intrusion, register_separation, phrase_regularity, off_beat_ratio, avg_phrase_length, motif_recurrence) shows **Hikichi's pattern on 16/17 uncredited tracks**. The exception is Death Egg Zone Act 1 (Ogawa match: Lydian mode, phrase_regularity=1.0 — the only Lydian track in S3K).

However, this is **sequencing attribution, not compositional authorship.** Features like register_separation (channel spacing), off_beat_ratio (SMPS quantization), and note_density are controlled by the sequencer. Hikichi's implementation habits stamp themselves onto every track he sequenced regardless of original composer. Given Cube was credited for only 8 tracks and Hikichi remembers composing some subset of those, the 16/17 result reflects **how many tracks Hikichi sequenced**, not how many he composed.

**The arranger bias operates at both layers:**
- Layer 1 (FM/Causal) → Setsumaru/Kashima programmer fingerprint
- Layer 2 (Symbolic) → Hikichi sequencer fingerprint
- Neither layer cleanly surfaces the original composer

**What survives arrangement:** mode/key selection, primary melodic interval relationships, harmonic vocabulary. These are the only features with any claim to compositional authorship, and they are underdeveloped in the current symbolic feature set.

## Phase 4 Findings — Full Corpus Calibration and Multi-Test Probes

### Fingerprint Rebuild

After indexing the complete VGM/SPC library for all 9 composers, fingerprints were recomputed from the full analyzed corpus. The Phase 1–3 fingerprints were built from 14–102 track subsets. The full corpus changed two dominant modes:

| Composer | Phase 1–3 mode | Phase 4 mode | Corpus | Format |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Miyoko Takaoka | Ionian | Phrygian | 175 SPC + 19 VGZ | SNES + MD |
| Masanori Hikichi | Phrygian | Dorian | 121 SPC + 28 VGZ | SNES + MD |
| Masayuki Nagao | Mixolydian | Mixolydian | ~57 VGZ | MD |
| All others | (various) | (unchanged) | — | MD |

Mode divergence (cases where mode-filtered ≠ distance-filtered winner) improved from 87.5% → 55%. **Nagao's Mixolydian is the most stable invariant in the corpus** — unchanged across every fingerprint version.

### Test 1 — 9-Way Euclidean Distance

Winner tally across all 58 tracks (uncredited only) with full corpus fingerprints:

| Composer | Wins |
|---|---|
| Masanori Hikichi | 15 |
| Masayuki Nagao | 12 |
| Yoshiaki Kashima | 11 |

MJ/Buxer tracks do not resolve correctly — expected, no MJ/Buxer calibration data. The arranger bias still pushes Hikichi to the top of distance wins.

### Test 2 — Takaoka Bonus Stage Hypothesis

Takaoka confirmed she composed a bonus stage that went unused and did not recognize any of the three final bonus stages. The forum hypothesis (Marble Garden and Magnetic Orbs share "simulated rhythm guitar" + jazz-funk scale) was tested directly:

| Track | d from MGZ Act 1 | Mode | Mode matches MGZ? |
|---|---|---|---|
| Magnetic Orbs | **3.095** | Ionian | Yes |
| Slot Machine | 3.342 | Mixolydian | No |
| Gumball Machine | 3.686 | Dorian | No |

Magnetic Orbs is the closest to Marble Garden Act 1 in both distance and mode. However, in the 9-way composer comparison, **Nagao wins all three bonus stages**; Takaoka is 2nd closest on Gumball (d=3.72). The hypothesis is **weak** — Senoue confirmed all three final bonus stages. If Takaoka's stage went unused and was replaced by Senoue's versions, no analysis of the existing finals can confirm or deny her composition.

### Test 3 — Mode-Survival Test (Arranger Bias Bypass)

Mode analysis bypasses the Hikichi sequencing fingerprint. Results on uncredited tracks: 13 same / 16 different compared to distance-only winner.

**The Flying Battery / Sandopolis / Endless Mine cluster:**

| Track | Mode | Mode → | Distance → |
|---|---|---|---|
| Flying Battery Act 1 | Mixolydian | Nagao | Hikichi |
| Flying Battery Act 2 | Mixolydian | Nagao | Hikichi |
| Sandopolis Act 1 | Mixolydian | Nagao | Hikichi |
| Sandopolis Act 2 | Mixolydian | Nagao | Hikichi |
| Endless Mine | Mixolydian | Nagao | Hikichi |
| Invincibility S&K | Mixolydian | Nagao | Hikichi |

All six are Mixolydian (Nagao's dominant mode), but Euclidean distance places them closer to Hikichi. This is the strongest structural evidence of split authorship: **Nagao composed, Hikichi sequenced** — Hikichi's implementation fingerprint sits on top of Nagao's harmonic content. Total uncredited Mixolydian tracks: 15. Nagao said he "produced half the music for Sonic 3" — 15 of ~30 uncredited Sega Sound Team tracks is consistent with that claim.

### Test 4 — Chord Progression Jaccard

**Degenerate.** All Jaccard scores = 0.000. The progression_fingerprint token notation in S3K artifacts is incompatible with the composer pool token encoding. Kashima wins by default (smallest pool → lowest loss). Not usable until token format is normalized.

### Test 5 — Nagao vs Hikichi Discriminating Features

Features with the strongest Nagao/Hikichi separation (normalized gap > 0.3):
- `register_separation` — gap 0.802
- `chromatic_intrusion_rate` — gap 0.656
- `rhythmic_entropy` — gap 0.641

On these three features alone: **Hikichi wins 46, Nagao wins 12** uncredited tracks. Nagao's largest-margin wins:

| Track | Mode | Nagao advantage |
|---|---|---|
| Sub Boss S3 | Dorian | 1.001 |
| Competition Menu (final, MJ version) | Phrygian | 0.932 |
| Carnival Night Act 2 | Lydian | 0.904 |
| Staff Roll S3 | Mixolydian | 0.554 |
| Marble Garden Act 2 | Dorian | 0.496 |

The first three are confirmed MJ/Buxer or Takaoka tracks — Nagao winning on the discriminating features for those confirms the features are **not sequencer-contaminated** (Hikichi didn't sequence them). These reflect genuine compositional-style overlap with Nagao. All confirmed Hikichi tracks pass the sanity check (Hikichi wins Mushroom Hill Act 1 & 2, Boss Act 2).

### Test 6 — DCP Seam Behavior

Seam type distribution across 58 tracks: SHARP 1 / MODERATE 30 / SOFT 26 / NONE 1.

**SHARP outlier — Competition Menu (final, MJ version):**
- `pre_seam_density_spike` = 14.05
- `seam_sharpness` = 13.39
- `loop_point_s` = 0.023 (23 ms from loop start to return)

A 23ms loop point is a near-instant circular collapse — the MJ team structured this track around a sample-based loop architecture where the "seam" is nearly imperceptible. The extreme spike/sharpness values are artifacts of measuring a compression point at 23ms, not a compositional technique. The structure is incompatible with SMPS-native loop architecture used by Sega composers.

Composer seam signatures from confirmed tracks:

| Composer | avg spike | avg sharp | Seam types |
|---|---|---|---|
| Miyoko Takaoka | 3.22 | 1.62 | MODERATE ×2 (highest of confirmed composers) |
| Masanori Hikichi | 2.18 | 0.29 | SOFT ×2, MODERATE ×1 |
| MJ/Buxer | 1.93 | 0.33 | SOFT ×3, MODERATE ×2 |
| Brad Buxer | 2.16 | 0.79 | MODERATE ×2, SOFT ×1 |
| Jun Senoue | 1.43 | 0.34 | SOFT ×3 |

**Staff Roll S&K** — NONE seam (spike=0.00, disappear=−7.079). Extreme negative disappearance index: voices are *added* at the loop return rather than dropped. Structurally anomalous in the corpus.

### Test 7 — Arranger Contamination Ranking

Feature contamination measured as: how often does each feature pull attribution toward Hikichi on confirmed non-Hikichi tracks? Four features are contaminated (>60% of non-Hikichi confirmed tracks are closer to Hikichi):

| Feature | Hikichi pull % | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| `register_separation` | 83% | Sequencer channel-spacing habit |
| `sustained_ratio` | 100% | Note-off timing habit |
| `bass_rhythmic_regularity` | 67% | Bass quantization grid |
| `groove_score` | 83% | SMPS tick grid influence |

Clean features (not contaminated): `pitch_entropy`, `chromatic_intrusion_rate`, `rhythmic_entropy`, `off_beat_ratio`, `motif_recurrence`, `melody_bass_independence`, `harmonic_tension_score`, `prevalence_of_tritones`, `vertical_dissonance`.

**9-way comparison using clean features — 11/41 uncredited tracks change attribution:**

Notable shifts:
- **Doomsday Zone:** Hikichi → Nagao (clean)
- **Death Egg Act 2:** Kashima → Takaoka (clean)
- **Desert Palace:** Hikichi → Kashima (clean)
- **Hydrocity Act 2:** Hikichi → Senoue (clean)
- **Invincibility S&K:** Hikichi → Senoue (clean)
- **Carnival Night Act 1 Beta:** Hikichi → Kashima (clean)

Clean attribution tally (uncredited): Hikichi 13, Kashima 11, Senoue 10, Nagao 3, Takaoka 1, Sawada 1, Setsumaru 1, Ogawa 1.

**Kashima's emergence as second-strongest clean attractor is significant.** He programmed the entire sound driver — his fingerprint is expected at the driver/FM layer — but the clean features (which suppress sequencer habits) still cluster strongly to Kashima, suggesting genuine compositional overlap beyond driver implementation.

## Sonic 3D Blast Connection

Sonic 3D Blast (Genesis, 1996) uses the same SMPS sound driver as S3K and shares two composers — **Tatsuyuki Maeda** and **Jun Senoue** — with confirmed individual track credits. This makes 3D Blast the primary calibration corpus for Maeda and Senoue fingerprints. Cross-game FM fingerprint consistency validated at fm_zero ±3%, detune ±0.3 across both games.

The 3D Blast ROM contains internal composer data for some tracks — the source for several Sonic Retro credit confirmations (Game Over, Continue, Stage Clear, 1UP, Gumball Machine = Jun Senoue; Chaos Emerald = Yukifumi Makino).

## What We Are NOT Doing

- Not doing AI-based composer attribution guesswork — Phase 1 & 2 structural fingerprinting relies solely on quantitative compositional bounds (entropy, harmonic tension, quantization).
- Not treating the three layers as independent — the goal is cross-layer structural signals that survive format/encoding differences.
- Not building a general music AI — this is invariant extraction for a specific, well-constrained corpus.
